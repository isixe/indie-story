It's been exactly **two years** since I went full-time indie last April. Looking back: I failed to turn indie development into a sustainable income and a freer life. But I did build a product I'm genuinely proud of — it got featured on the AppStore homepage and earned recognition from real users. So it wasn't all for nothing.

On the business side, it was a complete failure. For the past few months, average monthly revenue was just over 3000 RMB. My designer partner Dadian and I split 50/50, so I took home about 1500 — barely enough for takeout.

![](/images/appstore-featured-still-3k/revenue-data.jpeg)

But honestly? I see life as a process. As long as you gave it your all in each period, that's what counts. You can't negate the entire journey just because you didn't get the outcome you wanted. If you only care about results, well — everyone dies eventually. Life shrinks to a few data points, and all the joy is gone. Dadian and I agreed on this: it's a shame, it's regrettable. We couldn't close the ideal loop — build a product users love with great UX → users happily pay → word-of-mouth spreads → make an even better product.

On this two-year anniversary, we've decided to stop full-time indie development. The core reason: in the "small but beautiful" app space, we simply couldn't sustain ourselves through individual effort alone (our abilities are limited). **I'll include my contact info at the end — if you know of any job openings, referrals are very welcome.** Below I'll share why this path didn't work for us, along with lessons learned. Hope this helps anyone considering or currently doing indie dev.

## About Us: Worker Widget

Let me introduce us. Our core team is 3 people — me (Fugui) handling product decisions and iOS development, designer Dadian, and a part-time illustrator named Youxian.

![](/images/appstore-featured-still-3k/worker-widget-intro.png)

iOS development is mature — every conventional app category is saturated. Being just one person, I didn't want anything too complex. So I picked the iOS widget track (a then-new feature) combined with a niche direction, differentiating through better design. Looking around the indie dev scene, most were solo programmers or PMs building functional tools. Few emphasized design differentiation. Dadian and I both wanted to make something beautiful, so we went with this angle.

Another reason for design-focused differentiation: copy protection. A solo dev can't build moats through features alone. If your product idea takes off, clones pop up overnight. But if your edge is design — as long as nobody pixel-copies you — you're fine. Design is about taste; different designs attract different audiences. No direct competition.

Even so, we both went back to our day jobs after the initial push. The turning point came when I met Youxian, an illustrator. I told him about my idea — a countdown-to-quitting-time widget — and said maybe we could even make money from it. Youxian was stuck at home during COVID and had time. So he'd draw during the day, and I'd review his work after work, giving feedback. After a month of this back-and-forth, we had our first illustrated theme for Worker Widget. All three of us were thrilled — it felt like completing a real piece of work. We knew we had to turn this into a proper app.

![](/images/appstore-featured-still-3k/first-illustration.jpeg)

Soon after launch, AppStore's "App of the Week" picked us up — huge encouragement. We also found our monthly retention exceeded 50%. In those early days with minimal features, half our users were still around after 3 months. We thought this product had real potential. I couldn't resist the urge anymore — I quit my job to work on it full-time.

![](/images/appstore-featured-still-3k/app-of-the-week.jpeg)

Six months later (October), the editors put us on the AppStore homepage feature. We were full of confidence — thought it was the beginning of something big. Turns out, it was the peak. At first only I was full-time; after the homepage feature, Dadian thought it might actually work and quit too.

![](/images/appstore-featured-still-3k/homepage-feature.png)

## What We Thought Indie Development Was

Dadian and I were both product idealists — and honestly, we had zero business experience. Influenced by indie dev stories that said "build something users love and you'll be fine," we believed that if we just focused on product quality, user experience, and beautiful design, money would follow naturally.

My imagined business model: acquire 100K users per year, 10% conversion to paid, average user value 24 RMB (our original lifetime price). That's 240K/year — 10K per person per month. Not a fortune, but growing as the user base expands. We weren't chasing wealth; this felt acceptable.

Another assumption: even if the first product didn't make money, the user goodwill would make our second product launch much easier. High probability of success on the second try.

At the time, this model seemed bulletproof. In reality? Completely different story. We were like two hothouse flowers imagining a frictionless world.

## Cognitive Error #1: Good Products Don't Need Marketing

"Just build a great product and you don't need marketing" — this might be the biggest misconception among indie developers. It's like asking a kid where rice comes from and hearing "from the supermarket."

Two reasons this misconception exists:

- Most indie devs are programmers who never touch marketing at work, so they don't see how much work it is. Same way PMs and designers think a feature is "just code" — blind men touching the elephant.
- Marketing is a completely separate skill from development/design. Its importance to product success isn't immediately obvious, so many devs skip it — the initial ROI seems low. They pour all their time into what they're good at: building.

Look at any real business — sales and distribution costs are baked into every product. You can't personally reach every potential user. You have to pay to reach them. Digital products are no different. You need to capture user attention to promote your product. Pushing product information to users requires work. This is real-world friction you can't ignore.

Only a few categories can skip user acquisition entirely:

- Supply-constrained: only you solve a critical pain point, so everyone comes to you (like DeepSeek — worldwide hype with zero ad spend).
- Inherently viral: each user naturally brings others, with a viral coefficient > 1 from the start (early social networks — you get an account, invite your friends).
- Timing/trend riding: during certain periods, a category gets natural attention (mini gold rushes). If your product is good during the window, platforms feed you traffic.

Bottled water example: it's a commodity, yet every region has different brands. In theory the best-tasting water should dominate every shelf. Why doesn't it happen? Because products need delivery to reach users. Being good isn't enough — you also need the ability to push the product to people.

## Cognitive Error #2: Users Will Pay for Good Products

We subconsciously believe "you get what you pay for." Make a good product → good price → done.

But this is only part of the picture, especially for software. Quality is just one factor in revenue.

"Good software" is subjective. Same feature, one dev writes 500 lines in a day, another writes 5000 in 10 days. The latter costs 10x more, but users don't perceive a 10x quality difference.

A more complete model for user payment (value perception):

**Payment result = Product Quality × Perceived Value × Spending Habits**

Quality is up to the creator, but perceived value and spending habits can only be observed and adapted to. Never try to "educate" your users.

Perceived value = what users think your product is worth. No matter how hard you worked or how many features you cram in, users decide the price.

Utility tools are easiest to value. "I used to spend 4 hours on this; now I do it in 2. If my hourly rate is 100 RMB, any tool under 200 is worth it." Selling shovels to gold miners is a good business — but you won't be the only one selling shovels, so delivery still matters.

Beyond functional value, there's emotional value. Think of functional value as painkillers — emotional value is vitamins. Nice to have, but if you charge for it? Maybe not.

Example: phone wallpapers. A wallpaper app charges subscription for all wallpapers. What's it worth? To some, zero. To others, 10 RMB — or 50.

And spending habits: some people won't pay 1 RMB for a wallpaper, but will watch 60 seconds of ads. The wallpaper has value to them — they'll pay with attention or social shares — but not cash.

So **your product can be excellent, but the category and target audience may make it impossible to earn money**. Users pile on likes and encouragement, but the moment you show a price tag, they're gone. When users say "stay true to your vision," they often mean "stay free, keep coding for love." I've heard many examples of the same product failing in China but seeing 3-6x LTV uplift when pivoted to Western markets. Same product — different soil.

I constantly see posts asking "is there a clean, simple XX app?" What they really want isn't a cleaner app — it's a *free* app. Budgeting apps have existed since day one of the App Store. What features are users missing? If your goal is to build the best free budgeting app — go ahead. You'll be charity-developer #1.

## Charge From Day One: Paid Users Validate Real Demand

If your product needs revenue, charge early. Even 1 RMB. Unless your model is ad-supported.

What proves a product has user value? Users paying for it.

If you ask users whether they want a feature, their "yes" may not be genuine. But if you say "this feature costs money" and they still pay — that's real.

Charging validates genuine market demand. If you're building for a niche high-frequency vertical, early charging shows you which features matter to core users. Only the needs of the 5% who actually pay truly matter.

Charging also reveals your potential business model — whether it's worth investing more time. Some products have such poor conversion that 2-3 months of dev is enough. But if you blindly build for six months before thinking about payment, you've wasted a lot of time.

Calculator app example: if you build basic arithmetic + pretty skins, conversion will be terrible. Calculators have low emotional value for most people, and usage frequency is low. Without payment signals, you can't intuit which scenarios have market value.

You need specific scenarios with willing high-frequency payers. A random calculator tool I found: it does mortgages, car loans, personal taxes. For a user, paying 10 RMB to find optimal loan timing and save tens of thousands? That's a clear value anchor for payment.

But for individuals it's still low-frequency. The real paying users are real estate agents and car salespeople — they calculate loan plans daily. Instead of scribbling on paper, they generate plans from the app and send screenshots to clients. A much cleaner, more professional workflow. To them, 18 or 38 RMB is both acceptable.

![](/images/appstore-featured-still-3k/loan-calculator.jpeg)

Many worry charging might hurt reputation. It might. But accept this early: you can't please everyone. If you must choose, let the people who pay for you like you. Focus on your core users.

Others feel guilty charging for an imperfect product. But the payment gap is binary — 0 vs 1. Start low and raise the price later. In my experience, paying users are price-tolerant. Going from 8 to 18 RMB won't significantly affect conversion for the top 5% potential payers. For non-payers, even 1 cent is too much.

## Why Worker Widget Couldn't Take Off

All the cognitive errors above played out in product execution.

Marketing effort from Dadian and me? Essentially zero. We told ourselves to stick with what we're good at. When we tried writing Xiaohongshu posts, they flopped after a day's work, so we gave up (avoidance).

I assumed once we hit 100K users, word-of-mouth would sustain growth. Reality: unless your product is inherently viral or occupies search keywords, organic spread is minimal — especially for a low-frequency category. Our daily organic new user count averaged ~40. Think about it: I like a product, but I have no motivation to shout about it to everyone. And each person's influence circle is small. For mass-market products (not niche verticals), the viral coefficient is tiny. I'd guess you need 500K+ users before organic spread becomes viable.

We were also wildly optimistic about payment. Look at our App Store rating in China: 4.9 from 3000+ reviews. Our lifetime membership is 48 RMB. For a paid app, that's incredibly high.

We assumed such high satisfaction would mean high conversion. I only launched paid membership in month 9 of full-time dev. Even then, we were nervous.

![](/images/appstore-featured-still-3k/app-rating-4-9.jpeg)

The results? No fundamental difference.

Initial membership purchase rate (zero gated features): ~1.5%. After gating some peripheral features: ~3.5%. After gating 60% of widget themes: ~5%.

We wondered if we needed more features. We spent a month building a custom title-text widget with 20+ themes — surely there'd be something users liked. We made itmembership-only. Conversion from this widget? 3.5%. When users saw the paywall, 96.5% walked away.

![](/images/appstore-featured-still-3k/conversion-rate-chart.png)

The imagined 10% conversion was completely unreachable. An industry insider told me typical paid app conversion ranges from 2-7%. Our 5% was just a normal distribution result. It had nothing to do with product quality anymore — category and audience determine your conversion range. Choosing the right field matters.

We also thought having a user base would make launching a second product easier. Nope. We added a splash screen promoting our new app (Yali Altitude). Click-through to AppStore was ~20%. Download conversion after that depends on the new app's page — I'll assume 10%. So 20% × 10% = 2% conversion from existing users. And since widgets are low-frequency — only 10% of our users open the app monthly — we had no reliable channel to reach them all. Plus, just because someone uses your app doesn't mean they care about *you* or your next product.

## First Principles of Indie Development

By now you probably understand why we stopped. Our product's per-user value was too low to justify acquisition costs. We had no self-promotion skills and no platform tailwinds. As a hobby after work — earning a few thousand a month — it's fine. So we might continue as a side project.

My first-principles view of indie development:

> **Product Cost + User Acquisition Cost > User Value (LTV)**

Nothing profound — just basic business.

Break it down: say your per-user acquisition cost is 3 RMB (already low), and your payment conversion is 5%.

Your product price: 3 ÷ 0.05 = 60 RMB. But you can't spend all revenue on acquisition, and platforms take a cut (Apple's small developer fee is 15%). So your price should be 68+ RMB to make the math work. That's why many indie apps price at 68-98 RMB — it's normal. The same product feels more comfortable overseas where 88-168 RMB equivalent prices are acceptable (though acquisition costs are higher too). Subscription models are even better.

Three sustainable user acquisition paths:

- Paid user revenue covers acquisition costs
- Organic SEO from keywords people search daily — ASO is the same game. Or you solve a niche problem that people search for, and answers point to your app.
- Personal social media hustle — daily content, showing off work, driving attention to your products. (Hard. Results are unpredictable.)

## Maybe You Really Need a Personal Brand

The "super individual" and "personal brand" talk is everywhere online. From my perspective, it's genuinely the least risky entrepreneurial path for ordinary people.

A super individual is essentially a content creator with traction. Indie devs are natural content creators — using that to build trust with users, then converting attention into product traction, is a solid approach. If your social media only promotes your product, conversion will be terrible — who wants to follow an ad bot?

But if you actually have the ability to build a personal brand, you quickly hit a paradox: if I can make money just by creating content, why go through the pain of building a product? With 100K followers, you can run ads, promote affiliate courses, or create paid content. Why build something as heavy as an app?

Still, having a social media account and building trust with users does more good than harm. That's probably why so many people #buildinpublic.

## Final Advice

From validating an idea → building a shippable product → accumulating users → making money — there are so many unknowns. Unless you're desperate, start as a side project. Your mental health will thank you.

If you're thinking about indie dev: you can definitely build the product. I've never seen anyone fail at step one. The hard part is *selling* it — making money from it. From day one, think about distribution and monetization.
