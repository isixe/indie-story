## About Me

For those who don't know me, I should probably start with a brief introduction.

You can call me by my online handle Axiaoxin ([axiaoxin](https://axiaoxin.com/)). Originally from Sichuan, now based in Shenzhen, Guangdong.

I used to work as an internet programmer, mainly doing server-side development — just another guy writing code. Now I'm a freelancer, still writing code. I got into programming by chance in my senior year of high school when I stumbled upon Python, and I've been building small tools and websites ever since. The only regret is that I never knew how to market them back then, and I didn't have the awareness to do so, so most of them didn't last.

Despite the "xiao" (meaning "little") in my handle, I'm not young at all — I was born in 1990, about to turn 34. At 34, I'm already considered a "senior programmer" in the tech industry (just kidding).

I graduated from university in 2014 — that's already 10 years ago. Makes you wonder, how many 10 years do we have? After graduating, I worked at both small startups and big-name companies like Tencent.

After all the disillusionment, getting knocked around by society a few times, and growing tired of the repetitive grind, something just clicked. Life is short. I should put my energy into building my own core assets that generate passive income, so I can actually experience life — instead of memorizing interview trivia, grinding LeetCode, and spending countless hours preparing for interviews like looking for a needle in a haystack. No matter how well you perform, no matter how high your salary, you're still just a cog in the machine.

The reason I chose freelancing as an indie developer is a bit embarrassing: my company didn't renew my contract, so I was forced into it. But my situation is a bit unique — my indie development journey has run alongside my entire career, not something I started from scratch after being laid off. That gave me a lot more breathing room compared to other indie developers. My approach has been more about maintaining stability and finding breakthroughs rather than starting from nothing.

A few years ago, I came across a Douyin video of a guy picking trash near a construction site. He said there were factories all around him, but he'd rather pick trash than work in one. "You can't leave the factory because you don't have the skills to survive on your own," he said. "You have to take that step." He emphasized that he wasn't just picking trash — he was training himself to survive independently. If he could support himself without the factory, he could break free from that fate.

Aren't programmers the same? But we never realize it. That's what our education does — teaches us to obey, but not how to survive.

I used to hate putting myself out there, thought it was cringe. But now I've changed — I'm just another ordinary person. I want to promote myself, but I've realized I don't know how, and I'm embarrassed to try.

I've had my X (Twitter) account since 2012. It was always locked and I didn't let anyone follow me. Recently I decided to start using it properly — partly because X has high domain authority so shared links rank well, and partly to share indie dev content.

I really admire those indie devs who write weekly updates. I've been following one called "Dao Ge" who writes every week. I'm more of a casual note-taker.

### Income: Peak in Month One

In my first full month, I shared my income breakdown. My main sources were Google AdSense and self-media platform revenue. Little did I know month one would be the peak — income never topped that first month again. That was demoralizing, making me feel like everything I'd done in the past six months was useless. It's not just that income didn't grow — some months it actually declined continuously. I felt like I'd be forced back to a regular job soon. Anxious and helpless, because what you earn has nothing to do with what you're currently doing. What you do now only affects future income, not present income. You put in the work but see no results. Feeling powerless, small, weak, and helpless.

![Income fluctuation chart](/axiaoxin-indiehacker-6month/income-chart.png)

Fortunately, income has started to bounce back recently — a reassuring sign. Overall, these six months have been a rollercoaster. At the lowest point, I barely managed to keep my income in five figures. That's a milestone in itself. I hope it holds. If five figures is my floor, I can face problems with a calmer mindset. Six months is too short a window — seasonal traffic fluctuations might not fully show. I need to observe through a full year. If I can sustain this, then it's truly stable.

Here's a transparent look at this month's pending income — not much, but enough:

![Monthly income screenshot](/axiaoxin-indiehacker-6month/monthly-income.png)

I might be one of the few people hoping the Fed doesn't cut rates. At my peak, I was making nearly 280 USD a day. I can't hit that anymore, but it showed me I have that potential — and that's what keeps me going.

Sometimes all we need is a belief. When I feel like I can't keep going, I tell myself: you can do this, you can do this.

Short-term tactics can bring some traffic, but it never lasts. If you rely on short-term traffic hacks, you haven't really escaped the "work stops, income stops" trap — it's not true passive income. So I don't want to invest too much energy there.

WeChat Official Account income is now under 10 RMB a day — also cut in half. CPS affiliate revenue from the website has been cut again and again. Money is hard to make. I originally planned to give myself a monthly paycheck, but I stopped after two months — too lazy to keep it up.

When I got my severance package, I set a 100,000 RMB threshold for my account. If I burned through it in a year, I'd go back to work. Half a year in, I haven't touched it.

I define my indie dev path as more about stability and breakthrough, which makes me relatively conservative. Maybe I should set a six-month income target and force myself to earn enough to avoid going back to work — that might be more motivating. But I also don't want to pressure myself that much.

### Work: All Over the Place

In terms of daily task management, I currently feel scattered and easily distracted, making it hard to make meaningful progress across multiple projects.

When I first went full-time, my original plan was to develop a mini-program. But during development, I realized my JavaScript was rusty, so I started learning JS. The thing is, I have this quirk — I feel like I need to produce something tangible from learning, turn it into my own asset. So I started writing blog posts while learning. That started innocently enough, but soon the blog became the main focus.

New ideas keep popping up, and I'm afraid of forgetting them, so I jump on them immediately. This pulled me in more and more directions.

I have a ton of unrelated daily tasks: building website features, SEO (on-page and off-page), writing WeChat articles, making short videos, blogging, learning mini-program development — the list goes on. I'm constantly running, busy as hell, but without meaningful progress on any single project.

This is part of why I wanted to write this summary — to recalibrate my original goals and strategy. Without a clear, well-defined target, I always feel like I have unfinished tasks hanging over me, making it impossible to work in peace.

Moving forward, I'll focus on website development. I'll concentrate on completing one site's development phase and content filling before starting the next. The blog will be updated frequently for notes and ideas, while I'll only spend minimal time on self-media and external platforms.

Once I've finished the websites I want to build, I'll turn to self-media. After all, energy is limited — spreading it thin makes it hard to get positive feedback.

I used to clock in and write daily reports on WeCom, but I've stopped now — I kept forgetting, and it felt too formalistic.

My daily routine is completely out of whack now (heartbreaking). I think I need to restart the check-ins. As for daily reports, I've switched to posting on Weibo under the hashtag #IndieDevDiary. I've also opened up my X account to share updates there.

Looking back, most of my energy over the past six months went into off-page SEO and self-media — neither of which produced much results. A bit discouraging, but I haven't given up entirely. SEO is never immediate — you plant the seeds, and whether they sprout is up to fate.

One thing I've reflected on: my self-media positioning is unclear, and I haven't thought it through. Initially, I wanted to use self-media to drive traffic to my website, but it's tough — content often gets flagged as violating rules, and compliant content has low conversion rates. So I shifted strategy to monetizing through self-media directly, but growing followers is hard. Now I'm not even sure what my goal is — am I building a self-media brand, or just using it for traffic? The core issue is I haven't taken self-media seriously. I've been churning out low-quality auto-generated videos because I don't want to appear on camera — which means I can't produce high-quality content, content I wouldn't even watch myself. Maybe now isn't the right time for self-media short videos. I've scaled back updates and shifted focus back to website development.

When it comes to off-page SEO, I never really figured out the right approach. Many Chinese platforms ban external links, making traffic redirection nearly impossible. When I couldn't drive traffic, I pivoted to earning ad revenue directly on those platforms. But that created a dilemma: if I post content on platforms, my website content becomes less unique. So I've been overly cautious. On platforms that do allow links, I've had accounts banned one after another — Zhihu, Baidu, Douban. Xiaohongshu and WeChat Official Account have been flagged multiple times. This has made me realize: even though self-media platforms can generate income, they're not real assets. If a platform suddenly changes its rules or bans my account, it's game over. Better to focus on building my own website.

Half a year of hitting these potholes — not completely wasted. You can't truly understand without getting your hands dirty. "Get your hands dirty."

Moving forward, I'll recalibrate my positioning and keep going. I still haven't hit my small goal of 0.50 USD per day in blog ad revenue. I recently launched a new site — XiaoErZhuan. Right now, the initial development phase is done, but I still need to fill in the content before I can call it complete. Beyond content for the blog and XiaoErZhuan, my next tasks include building the API service for my planned website, adding features that can generate independent income. I don't necessarily have to monetize right now, but I need to have the capability to do so. As for self-media... I'll do it when I feel like it.

### Life: Adaptation and Anxiety

Honestly, life hasn't changed that much. I'm at home typing on my keyboard almost every day. I go out occasionally. Exercise still isn't part of my routine.

I visited Jieyang in March, Guizhou in June, and Guangzhou in July. I also moved in July — from the worker-bee paradise of Nanshan, Shenzhen to the old streets of Dongmen, Luohu. Now I can step out and hit the shopping street, but I barely go downstairs more than a few times a month. My life is basically sitting in front of a computer, clacking away.

Social security and housing fund are sorted. After careful consideration, I decided to keep paying into the pension system even though I initially thought about stopping. I'm currently paying into social security as a flexible employee. Since I'm receiving unemployment benefits, I only need to cover the pension portion myself — medical insurance is covered by the unemployment fund. I applied for 10 months of unemployment benefits, which means about 2,000+ RMB per month, with 4 months remaining.

I used to check in daily on WeRead, but since going full-time indie, I somehow have less time for reading — not the state I wanted. I've recently started reading again. Many of the free books I'd saved have become paid. I just finished my 100th book on WeRead the other day.

After moving, I set up a new workspace — a standing desk and an ergonomic chair. It almost feels like a man's vanity table.

Six months in, I'm not entirely sure I've fully adapted to this lifestyle, but judging by how things are going, I'm doing okay.

Before this, my two biggest worries were: first, how low could my income really go — would it be enough to live on? And second, would my family understand? Neither concern has materialized, so things are going relatively smoothly.

The only real issue was the psychological shock of the income cliff at the beginning — losing that sense of security. But over time, it bothered me less. My mom always says, "as long as it's enough." Having that mindset is actually really hard. "Enough now" doesn't mean "enough later," so I'm not ready to coast.

Now that I'm earning on my own without a company backing me, even if it's less, I don't envy people with high salaries — because I can have income without going to an office. That's not something to shout from the rooftops; I should keep a low profile. But that conflicts with the need to market myself — another balance I haven't figured out.

There have been moments of anxiety over these six months. Full-time indie development doesn't just mean building your product — you birth it, and you raise it. You're not just a developer; you're also a tester, sysadmin, product manager, designer, marketer, and customer support. It's not enough to just build it — what matters more is how to make it generate income, how to market it, how to acquire users. These pressures are something employees never experience. As an employee, you just show up, sit down, and focus on your piece of the puzzle. You're only one part of the chain, and you don't need to worry about the whole thing. But as an indie developer, if you can't close the loop and generate income, you won't survive — and it's serious. So anxiety is real.

When anxiety hits, I get irritable, and that makes me impatient with family. I want to validate my ideas as quickly as possible, and I hate anything that distracts me — even things like mopping the floor, dusting the table, or bringing in the laundry. None of that matters to me. Some days I don't even notice what's on the floor. This has led to friction with my wife. But it's not that I'm trying to push chores onto her — I genuinely get into a flow state where I forget to eat three meals a day.

September might not be productive — I'm heading to Xinjiang on Friday. Work is for living, so I need to learn to reset my mindset. This process is a kind of growth. Time to fix my sleep schedule, my mindset, and start exercising. Worst case? I'll just go find another job. Relax.

## A Few Confusions

Over these six months, I've had several nagging questions I haven't fully figured out. I touched on some earlier, but I want to lay them out clearly to organize my thoughts.

### Confusion 1: Should I Double Down on Current Income or Diversify?

Google AdSense is my most stable income source and pays relatively well compared to others, but it's subject to traffic fluctuations and the ad market. Future uncontrolled factors could affect it. Relying on a single income channel is risky — especially when markets shift or policies change. Extreme example: what if Google AdSense shuts down? If I invested more time and energy into other channels, they might become stable passive income streams long-term and reduce overall risk.

I'm not sure if self-media platforms are worth the investment because even building an account from scratch is hard, let alone reaching stable passive income. My personal goal is to build a passive income system that doesn't require active management. While self-media can generate passive income, it's tied to platforms that could ban your account any day — you never truly control your core assets. Websites give me more control. Platforms like WeChat Official Account, Douyin (TikTok China), and Bilibili can bring massive traffic and exposure, but their policies and algorithms can change overnight, and account bans are a real risk — especially since content, speech, and ad strategies are often strictly scrutinized. You have zero control. If I pour all my energy into them and my account gets banned, everything is gone.

My current conclusion: maintain Google AdSense as my stable income foundation while gradually increasing investment in other channels to find sustainable growth points. Websites offer greater autonomy, but completely abandoning self-media could mean missing important traffic and opportunities.

### Confusion 2: Deep Focus on One Channel vs. Multiple Projects

Even if I choose to go all-in on Google AdSense, I still face a choice: do I focus on optimizing existing websites or keep building new ones to capture more traffic? It's the same core question — depth vs. breadth.

A deep-focus strategy reduces the mental cost of task-switching, leading to higher concentration and avoiding the "doing many things without going deep" feeling. But going all-in on one site feels painfully slow, like I'm missing out on potential traffic.

For now, I want to reduce the chaos and scattered energy, so I'm going with a "deep focus" strategy: concentrate on one website until it meets my goals, then move to the next. This should give me better focus and a stronger sense of accomplishment.

The practical plan:

- Finish one website project before starting the next, avoiding the energy drain of too many concurrent tasks.
- Pick an existing or new website, set clear milestones — like "complete SEO optimization and content filling this month" or "finish all core development and launch." Go all-in until its functionality or traffic hits the target, then move to the next project.

### Confusion 3: Should Indie Developers Publicize Their Income?

A lot of people are curious about "how to make money through indie development." Public income can be a hot topic that attracts an audience and builds your personal brand. But once you make it public, not only do you expose your financial situation, but you also invite competition or attacks from peers. Some people will be unhappy or jealous. Stay silent, and people doubt whether you're telling the truth.

Initially, I shared my income to prove myself — to show people around me and my family that I could make it without a job, and to prove to myself that I could do better. But looking back, that was driven by ego — I still cared too much about how others saw me. People with real inner strength don't need to prove anything to anyone. Just focus on doing your own work well.

### Confusion 4: Family Life Challenges of Full-Time Indie Development

I recently watched a show called "The Little Couple" about a big-tech programmer who loses his job and becomes a stay-at-home dad. The workplace plot was pretty unrealistic, but the protagonist's struggles as a stay-at-home dad — dealing with life issues and the judgmental looks from others — I'm not sure if it's accurate, but it felt plausible.

## Things I Want to Share

When people find out you're making a living from indie development, many will reach out to ask questions. But honestly, there's not much I can share that applies to everyone.

If I had to summarize, here are a few things:

### Is Indie Development Hard?

Of course it's hard. If it were easy, who would still go to an office job? Indie development should start while you're still employed, not after you lose your job.

Nothing you do today will show results tomorrow. You have to actually do it before results are possible.

Sure, some people hit the ground running and see immediate results, but they're the exception. I'm not naive enough to think I'm one of them.

Don't just analyze — you have to execute. If you have a stable job and a decent idea while you can still afford to take risks, go do it. Don't overthink whether it's worth it or if the market is saturated. Just do it. But for those who are struggling to make ends meet, you might need to plan more carefully — your time is limited and the pressure is real.

A lot of people will tell you not to do things that only make yourself feel good. I disagree. If you can't even move yourself, how can you move people like you — or anyone else?

I don't think about potential income before starting something. I just do what I enjoy. If it helps others and provides value, traffic will follow naturally. If not, it's still something I like — like a little ornament on my desk. That's enough for me.

Start from your interests, from your real-life needs. Build a product that solves a problem. Even if nobody else uses it, at least you can use it yourself. That's my approach.

The fact that I've survived doesn't mean my method is correct. It might be luck plus action — maybe survivor bias. I don't think my path is replicable, so take my insights with a grain of salt.

### Do What You're Best At, in the Way Others Need It Most

For me right now, making money revolves around what I do best. It's not an easy path.

Many people take jobs they don't love just to make a living, slowly losing themselves in the daily grind. But in modern society, success often comes from your unique traits. Passion and making money aren't opposites — they can go hand in hand, even merge. But it takes deliberate practice.

I believe the best approach is to take what you're best at and do it in the way others need it most. More importantly, you just have to start doing it. Only through action can you discover what people truly need. A lot of people know the theory but never act. Building tools, creating websites, writing code — these are things I love. But satisfying my own interests isn't enough. They need to be useful to others to create real value.

Passion alone doesn't automatically turn into income. But through deliberate practice and continuous improvement, passion can become a valuable skill for others. Programmers who love coding have a clear advantage here — development experience isn't just about satisfying your own problem-solving urges; it's about making what you build valuable to the outside world.

### Learn to Embrace Loneliness and Manage Anxiety

These six months of indie development have felt lonely — lonely in body and mind. Working from home alone, it's hard to find peers in real life. No one truly shares your highs and lows — even my wife, who is also a programmer, doesn't seem genuinely interested when I share a clever implementation or a breakthrough.

So you have to get used to loneliness. Learn to embrace it. This is a long game. If you can't stand being alone, it's hard to keep going.

I'm a full-time indie developer with a stable income base and I still feel anxious. Imagine how it is for those starting from zero.

I've had many bouts of anxiety and confusion, but I've always managed to pull through. Going for a bike ride is a great way to reset when anxiety hits.

At this point, I have to drop a line from Beyond's song "Who Will Roam With Me":

> Only the stubborn know — tomorrow's road may be even more uncertain
> Tired of this, I've gone numb
> Stop dwelling on what's worth fighting for
> We're all just passing through

### Who Should NOT Go Full-Time Indie

If you can't fill a solid 8-hour day with your own tasks, there's no point going full-time.

If you expect to make money right away, it's not for you either. Indie development doesn't conflict with having a job. First, get a job. A stable paycheck will keep your mindset steady so you can do your best indie work. Only consider going full-time indie when your indie income can cover your living costs.

My hope is to get to a point where I have less and less to do — and then go find a security guard gig just for the experience.

### Learn to Prioritize

Everything can be prioritized. Many people can't identify what matters most. Priorities also shift over time — you need to adapt flexibly to your actual situation.

Learning to prioritize also applies to this very article. It might not be helpful to everyone, but those who find it valuable will find the key points they're looking for. The critical information and the underlying "way" are all here.
