As the capital machine grows larger, employees become tiny replaceable cogs in the system.

I'm Ran He, a frontend programmer from a major agricultural province in Henan, exploring the path of freelancing. I developed a browser extension full-time — Circle Reader. With one click it removes ads and clutter from web pages, giving you a cleaner, more comfortable reading experience.

Unlike most products on the market, it features a self-developed parsing engine and rendering engine that are smarter at identifying main content, faster at parsing, and produce more pleasing layouts. Download Circle for your favorite browser 👉 circlereader.com

## Getting Into Tech

One day in September 2011, the summer cicadas had just stopped their chorus, but the daytime heat still lingered. Lugging my suitcase, I walked alone into the freshman registration at Henan Polytechnic University, becoming a student there with my admission letter in hand.

My family's poor rural conditions meant I had to cover my own living expenses. From the very first month of enrollment, I started looking for all kinds of part-time jobs to make money. In the end, I not only missed out on club recruitment but also got scammed out of a lot of money. Then a flyer from a late-recruiting network technology club caught my attention, and from then on, a mechanical engineering major began studying computer science.

Every day, besides attending classes, I buried myself in the library reading all kinds of tech books. By my sophomore year, I could basically build a website on my own. Teachers who needed high-quality course websites gradually got to know me — word of mouth worked its magic. Every week, teachers would ask me to build them a site for 1,000 RMB each. I earned my first pot of gold. By graduation, I had redesigned the school's official website.

## Joining ByteDance

When I graduated in 2015, my poor hearing prevented me from getting my CET-4 English certificate. Every mechanical engineering company rejected my resume. Left with no choice, I turned to the internet and eventually joined a travel-tech company in Hangzhou. Back then I thought I'd lost at the starting line right after graduation — now I realize that every cloud has a silver lining.

I switched between a few more companies before finally landing at ByteDance.

## Why I Left ByteDance

To be honest, working at ByteDance was pretty great. Housing subsidies, afternoon tea, unlimited snacks, free meals — the list goes on. Here's why I considered leaving:

**Alternating six-day workweeks (now canceled)**

The alternating schedule — one week with a weekend, one without — was the hardest to tolerate. Sure, there was double pay, but working six days straight with just one day off felt no different from no break at all. Maybe it's fine for fresh graduates, but for a programmer in his thirties, it was really rough. Combined with the nature of development work, spending most of each day at the office completely drained the meaning from "going to work."

**The fate of aging programmers**

"Old programmers must pivot or be phased out" — the legendary saying in tech.

When you're young, you can pull all-nighters fixing bugs and shipping releases. But when you're older, married with a family, can you still pull all-nighters? From a company's perspective, isn't it more cost-effective to hire recent grads — lower salary, willing to work crazy hours?

**The desire for freedom**

One day online, I stumbled upon the remote work and indie developer community, and something stirred inside me.

In the Electric Duck community chat groups, I kept seeing people land satisfying remote jobs. They could work from anywhere, travel while working — wasn't this the life I'd imagined? After observing for a while, I realized their only real advantage was not being tied to an office. They still had product managers breathing down their necks and the endless annoyance of meetings. So I started looking into freelancing, and during countless sleepless nights, my own project began to take shape and grow wildly.

That's why I left ByteDance, giving up a six-figure salary. In short, I value freedom more. I don't like the constraints of big companies. I want to do what I love, on my own terms and my own schedule.

## The Seed of the Product

Once I decided to become a freelancer, I was constantly thinking about what kind of product could help me reach my goal. Actually, finding a project isn't hard — quite the opposite, it's simple. You just start from your own daily needs. My product is Circle Reader, and the idea came from a personal need that wasn't being met.

I usually browse through articles while eating — novels, news, tech, you name it. Foreign PC web pages aren't too bad, but most domestic Chinese sites are plastered with ads, have unfriendly layouts, and don't let you control font size or color. I wondered if there was a product that could strip away the ads and noise from a page, and ideally let me customize font sizes and more. I searched online and tried several products. None of them fully satisfied me — some got the content wrong, some missed parts of the article, some broke images, and so on. Eventually I decided to build my own product. It had to avoid all these problems while offering more flexible control over page styling.

After three months of reading all kinds of papers and algorithms, Circle Reader was born. It uses algorithmic content extraction to parse and display articles in milliseconds, while offering a wide range of options to control page styling and themes. Here's the default interface:

## Why I Went Full-Time

Once your MVP is ready, put it out there and let users tell you what to do next — that's my hard-earned advice.

At first, I only used it myself. My plan was to polish it into a perfect version before releasing it. After getting feedback from a few friends, I decided to launch it to the market. So in March of this year, I published it on the Chrome Web Store, and posted an introduction to Circle Reader on Appinn's discovery channel. The next day, the Appinn editor Qing Xiaowa featured it — and it blew up. Tons of installs poured in, passing 3,000 in just a few days. Demand for other browser stores grew quickly, so I published on Edge, Firefox, 360, and Opera.

Every day brought all kinds of feedback, eating up huge amounts of my time. Friends suggested I add a donation link — if users liked it, they'd donate. So in the second version, I added a donation link, and sure enough, people actually donated. That's when I realized this product was worth maintaining seriously. After researching competitors' revenue models, I decided to focus on this product full-time. Back then, I'd get home at 10 PM, wash up, lie in bed, and code until 2 AM, then head to work the next day as usual. With a new project kicking off at the company, I was completely drained — physically and mentally.

Finally, on May 20th, I submitted my resignation letter and began full-time development and maintenance of Circle Reader.

## Achievements

I'm sure you're most curious about my income, so let me share the current situation:

Circle Reader launched on Chrome in March. On March 3rd, I posted an introduction article on Appinn, and on March 4th it was featured on their front page. In early April, I rolled out to other browser stores. Currently it supports Chrome, Edge, Firefox, 360, and Opera — other Chinese browsers support manual installation. On June 30th, v2.0.0 launched with an account system. On August 5th, v2.1.0 introduced premium features. On August 30th, v2.2.0 added a help center and enhanced the keyboard shortcut system.

As of August 30th, Google Analytics shows over 30,000 users. The first version didn't include analytics, so I can't know the exact install count — my rough estimate is around 40,000 to 50,000.

Donation income totaled 950 RMB.

From the premium feature launch on August 5th to August 30th, 79 users purchased it, totaling over 3,000 RMB in revenue.

That's where things stand today.
