## Nights and weekends for 3 years. 47 products launched. $127 total revenue. The brutal math of indie hacking nobody shows you.

### The Tweet That Started Everything

December 2021. I was scrolling Twitter when I saw it:

“Just hit $10K MRR with my side project! Started 3 months ago. Quit my job next week 🚀”

The replies were insane:

- “Congrats king!”
- “This is inspiring!”
- “What tech stack did you use?”
- I looked at my bank account. Then at my job. Then back at Twitter.

“If he can do it, why can’t I?”

That thought destroyed the next 3 years of my life.

### Project #1: The One That Made Me Believe

**Idea**: A Chrome extension that blocks distracting websites during work hours.

**Time spent**: 2 weeks  
**Revenue**: $0  
**Downloads**: 47 (23 were my friends)

But here’s the thing — I was hooked.

Building it felt good. Seeing it work felt amazing. I didn’t care that nobody used it. I had built something from nothing.

“The next one will be different,” I told myself.

Narrator: It wasn’t.

### The Golden Era (Projects #2–15)

For the next year, I was unstoppable.

Every Saturday morning. Every Sunday afternoon. Some weeknights when I couldn’t sleep.

I built:

- A habit tracker (there are only 10,000 of these)
- A markdown note-taking app (Notion who?)
- A workout logger (revolutionary, I know)
- A budget tracker (Mint but worse)
- A URL shortener (because the world needed another)
- A password manager (I got scared and deleted it)
- A Pomodoro timer (with dark mode though)
- A weather app (yes, really)
- A todo list (my 4th one)

**Combined revenue**: $0  
**Combined users**: ~200 (mostly friends and family being nice)  
**Combined hours**: ~400 hours

Do the math. That’s **$0/hour**.

But I didn’t care about the money yet. I was learning. React, Node.js, databases, deployment, AWS.

Every project taught me something. And every project made me think: “The next one will be THE one.”

### The Pivot (Projects #16–30)

Around month 18, reality hit.

My girlfriend: “You’ve been building apps for over a year. How much money have you made?”

Me: “It’s not about the money. It’s about learning.”

Her: “How much?”

Me: “…Zero.”

Her: “Zero dollars? In 18 months?”

Me: “I made $12 once.”

Her: “How?”

Me: “Friend felt bad and bought me coffee as ‘payment’ for my help.”

That conversation hurt. Not because she was mean — she wasn’t. But because she was right.

I wasn’t building products. I was building hobbies.

So I pivoted. No more “scratch my own itch” apps. Real products. For real problems. That real people would pay real money for.

**Project #16**: A SaaS for freelancers to send invoices  
**Problem**: Freshbooks exists  
**Revenue**: $0

**Project #17**: A tool for developers to generate API documentation  
**Problem**: Swagger exists  
**Revenue**: $0

**Project #18**: A platform for designers to sell templates  
**Problem**: Gumroad exists  
**Revenue**: $0

**Project #19–30**: Same story, different features  
**Combined revenue**: $47 (one person paid for my freelance invoice tool then disputed the charge)

### The Desperation Phase (Projects #31–40)

Year 2. Still zero traction.

I started trying everything:

- Added AI features (before it was cool)
- Built a crypto thing (don’t ask)
- Made a no-code tool (ironic, I know)
- Created a course platform (who am I teaching?)
- Built a job board (for a niche with 400 people)
- Nothing worked.

But Twitter kept showing me the winners:

- “Hit $50K MRR!”
- “Bootstrapped to $1M ARR!”
- “Sold my SaaS for 6 figures!”
- Each success story felt like a personal attack.

“What do they have that I don’t?”

Turns out: **An audience**.

Every successful indie hacker I studied had one thing in common — they built in public. They had followers. They had distribution.

I had 47 Twitter followers. 31 were bots.

### The Final Push (Projects #41–47)

Year 3. One last sprint.

I decided to build AND market simultaneously. No more “build first, market later.”

**Project #41**: A newsletter tool  
**Marketing**: Posted on Twitter daily for a month  
**Followers gained**: 23  
**Revenue**: $0

**Project #42**: A link-in-bio tool  
**Marketing**: Posted on Reddit (got banned for self-promotion)  
**Revenue**: $0

**Project #43**: An analytics dashboard  
**Marketing**: Cold emailed 200 potential users  
**Revenue**: $15 (one month subscription, canceled after trial)

**Project #44**: A feedback widget  
**Marketing**: Posted on Product Hunt  
**Upvotes**: 12  
**Revenue**: $0

**Project #45**: A screenshot tool  
**Marketing**: Paid $100 for Twitter ads  
**Revenue**: $0

**Project #46**: A scheduling app  
**Marketing**: Posted in 15 Discord servers (got kicked from 8)  
**Revenue**: $35 (family member pity purchase)

**Project #47**: A bookmark manager  
**Marketing**: YouTube video (got 43 views)  
**Revenue**: $30 (my dad bought it. Twice. Different emails. He thought I wouldn’t notice.)

### The Math That Crushed My Soul

**Total time invested**: ~1,200 hours  
**Total revenue**: $127  
**Hourly rate**: $0.11/hour

For context, minimum wage in my state is $15/hour.

If I’d worked at McDonald’s for those 1,200 hours, I’d have made $18,000.

Instead, I made $127.

And $50 of that was from my dad.

### What I Actually Learned (The Painful Truth)

#### Lesson 1: Building Is the Easy Part

I thought the hard part was coding. Nope.

Marketing is 10x harder. Distribution is 100x harder. Getting someone to care is 1000x harder.

You can build the most elegant, bug-free, beautiful product in the world. If nobody knows it exists, it’s worth $0.

#### Lesson 2: "Scratch Your Own Itch" Is Terrible Advice

8 of my projects were solving my problems.

Turns out, I have weird problems that nobody else has.

Just because you need a “Pomodoro timer that integrates with your pet’s feeding schedule” doesn’t mean anyone else does.

#### Lesson 3: Ideas Are Worth Nothing. Execution Is Worth Nothing. Distribution Is Everything.

I had decent ideas. Some were good ideas. A few were great ideas.

I executed well. My code was clean. My UIs were polished. My apps actually worked.

But none of that matters if you can’t get it in front of people.

#### Lesson 4: You're Competing With Free

For every paid tool I built, there were 10 free alternatives.

Why would someone pay for my habit tracker when there are 100 free ones?

My answer: “Mine is better!”

The market’s answer: “We literally don’t care.”

#### Lesson 5: Timing Beats Everything

Some of my ideas were decent. But they were 2 years late or 2 years early.

I built a Zoom alternative in 2018. Nobody cared.
I built a remote work tool in 2022. Everyone already had Slack.

Timing isn’t everything, but it’s more than I thought.

#### Lesson 6: You Need an Audience BEFORE You Build

This was the big one.

Every successful indie hacker had an audience first. They built products for people who already followed them.

I was building products, then trying to find an audience.

That’s backwards.

Build an audience. Then ask them what they need. Then build that.

#### Lesson 7: Most "Successes" on Twitter Are Lies

After 3 years of failure, I started actually looking at the “success stories.”

“$10K MRR” guy? His MRR dropped to $2K after 2 months. He never tweeted about it.

“Quit my job” girl? She went back after 4 months. Deleted the tweet.

“Sold for 6 figures” dude? The sale fell through. He doesn’t mention it.

Twitter shows you the wins. Nobody posts their L’s.

#### Lesson 8: Side Projects Are Lottery Tickets

You know what my 47 projects taught me?

Most side projects fail. Even the ones by smart people. Even the ones with good ideas. Even the ones with marketing.

The ones that succeed? Luck plays a bigger role than anyone admits.

Right place. Right time. Right audience. Right problem.

You can increase your odds. But you can’t guarantee success.

### What I'd Do Differently

If I could go back to December 2021 and talk to myself:

**1. Build an audience first**  
Start a blog. Start a Twitter. Start a YouTube. Create value for free. Build trust. Then build products.

**2. Validate before building**  
Don’t spend 2 weeks coding. Spend 2 days selling a landing page. If nobody clicks “Pre-order,” don’t build it.

**3. Pick ONE thing**  
47 projects = 47 half-assed attempts. If I’d spent 1,200 hours on ONE project, maybe it’d be different.

**4. Charge from day one**  
Free is a terrible business model. If people won’t pay $5, they won’t pay $50 later.

**5. Ship ugly MVPs**  
Half my projects never launched because I was “polishing” them. Ship fast. Fix later. Most products die before anyone sees them.

### The Real Question: Was It Worth It?

$127 revenue. 1,200 hours invested. 47 failed projects.

Was it worth it?

Financially? Hell no.

Educationally? Actually… yes.

I learned:

- React, Node, Python, Docker, AWS, databases, APIs, deployment
- Marketing (even though I suck at it)
- Sales (even though I failed)
- Product thinking (even though nobody wanted my products)
- Shipping (even though nobody used what I shipped)
- That knowledge landed me a better job. $30K salary increase. Paid off in 5 months.

So technically, my side projects made me $30,000+.

Just not the way I expected.

### The Brutal Truth About Indie Hacking

Here’s what nobody tells you:

- Most indie hackers fail
- Most products make $0
- Most “overnight successes” took 3+ years
- Most Twitter success stories are survivorship bias
- Most people quit before they succeed

But some people do make it. Some projects do work. Some ideas do win.

The question is: Are you willing to build 47 products to find the one that works?

Because that might be what it takes.

### Where I Am Now

I stopped building side projects.

Not because I gave up. But because I learned the lesson:

Build an audience first. Build products second.

So now I write. I share what I learned. I help other developers avoid my mistakes.

Maybe this becomes something. Maybe it doesn’t.

But at least this time, I’m not starting from zero followers.
