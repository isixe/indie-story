Three months ago, I made a bold claim. I said I was going to run a social experiment on myself — to see if I could leave the traditional 9-to-5 and achieve a monthly income of 10,000 RMB within three months of indie hacking for the overseas market. I was riding high, buying up Cursor, Windsurf, Claude — everything I could get my hands on. I geared myself up to the teeth, talked big, and felt like I was sitting at the cutting edge of the AI wave. Aiming at America, jabbing at England, pointing at Germany, lunging at France — even Empress Dowager Cixi would've clapped in approval.

Three months later, all I want to do is call my past self an idiot. Just thinking about it makes me cringe hard enough to dig a three-bedroom apartment out of the ground. And 10K a month? Honestly, my biggest income right now is the 2,000 RMB monthly unemployment benefit from the government. After three months of indie hacking overseas, I've made a grand total of **$3 USD**!

Alright, enough self-flagellation. Let me do a simple retrospective of the mistakes I've made. Consider it me untangling my own thoughts. If it can offer even a sliver of insight to someone else, that's merit enough.

## Bragging and the Scarcity Mindset

The moment I boasted about making 10K a month in three months, the tragedy was probably already written. That single brag made me chase whatever seemed to have the best payoff, the quickest money. The time pressure heavily distorted my decision-making. I'd think I'd had a brilliant idea while falling asleep at night, and by daytime I'd be pounding out code, skipping any market research. After launch, I'd blast it everywhere — Product Hunt, Hacker News, Reddit. The next day I'd check the analytics and find a handful of Indians who'd stopped by for a quick look. Probably fellow devs checking to see if there was anything worth copying, only to find the cupboards bare.

My first five projects all followed this pattern. Looking back, beyond the approach, the core issue was mindset. I knew intellectually that you're supposed to validate demand first. But decades of workplace conditioning had wired me to believe that writing code, building something, was the real work. Validating demand, post-launch operations — those were things you filled in after the build was done. But in business logic, those things probably deserve *higher* priority than building.

Add the bragging on top, and I'd fallen into the trap of a *scarcity mindset*. The more I tried to force a miracle, the more twisted and stupid my decisions became. After seven websites launched and died within two weeks, my mentality started to crack. I felt wrong in every way, couldn't muster any spirit — like a neutered bull in a Wang Xiaobo novel.

I first came across the term "scarcity mindset" in the book *Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much*. The authors argue that when people are in a state of resource scarcity (time, money, or anything else), the brain automatically switches into "focus mode," obsessing over the scarce resource while neglecting everything else important.

That was me — overly fixated on short-term income targets, leading to distorted decisions. I knew I should validate demand first, but I dove headfirst into development anyway. I knew I should build slowly and steadily, but I kept chasing quick wins. This mindset trapped me in a vicious cycle: the more anxious I was, the worse I performed; the worse I performed, the more anxious I became.

The end result? I neither reached my goal nor burned myself out completely. Looking back now, I'd say: instead of setting yourself an unrealistic target, keep your feet on the ground and take it one step at a time. Entrepreneurship and going overseas is a marathon, not a sprint.

## Figuring Out *What* Matters More Than *How*

This is one of the crucial differences between working a job and doing indie hacking. At a job, your role is simple — you're a programmer, you implement things. But indie hacking, especially when you're relying on it to pay the bills, is fundamentally a *business*. And running a business isn't just about building something. You also need to find the right place to set up your stall, make your stall appealing, and know how to shout for customers. *Where* to set up, *how* to set it up, *how* to get attention — there's so much to figure out. And you need to be thinking about all of this *before* you start building. The *how* of building is just one link in a chain. Not unimportant, but among all the links, it's not the deciding factor — especially for indie developers, where the gap created by technology or product quality alone is actually pretty small.

For example, I built an AI avatar generation website. Technically it was trivial — call an API, add a frontend, add payment. Could knock it out in a day. But after launch, nobody came. Why? Because I'd never once thought about how people would discover it.

My thinking was naive: "This stuff is trending, people will use it." But in reality, there were already dozens of similar products on the market. Why would users pick mine over theirs? What was my differentiation? Where were my target users? What channels would they come through? I hadn't thought through any of these questions before diving in.

Later I realized that before building any product, you need to answer three questions clearly:

1.  What problem am I solving?
2.  Who will pay to have this problem solved?
3.  How do I find these people?

These questions seem simple, but truly answering them isn't easy. A lot of the time we think we've spotted a great opportunity, but we've only scratched the surface. Take the AI avatar project — on the surface, the market looks huge, but competition is already fierce. To break through in that space, having a working product isn't nearly enough.

## Don't Rush to Build — Figure Out Your Distribution First

This overlaps with the point above, but it's worth spelling out for my own sake.

Distribution is simply how you let people who need your thing know that your thing exists. You can use community channels, social media, organic SEO traffic, paid ads — all kinds of approaches. But which one actually fits *your* product? Start researching and you'll discover there are as many paths as there are products.

Take community channels. What overseas communities are there? Product Hunt, Hacker News, Reddit, Indie Hackers — the usual suspects. But each community has its own rules and culture, and you need to spend time understanding them.

Product Hunt is a launch platform. You need a polished product to post there. And ideally, you get a hunter to help you launch — going solo is basically throwing your product into the void. Timing matters too: Tuesday through Thursday mornings US time work best.

Hacker News is a tech community. Post an AI avatar generator there and people will think you're a grifter. But if you open-source something interesting, it might spark a discussion.

Reddit is the most complex — thousands of subreddits, each with its own rules. Post in the wrong place and you'll be deleted and banned in minutes. And Redditors *hate* marketing. If you're too blatant about promoting your product, get ready to be roasted.

So community marketing looks simple on the surface, but the nuances are endless. You need to immerse yourself in these communities, understand their cultures, build a reputation, and *then* think about promoting your product. That process can take months or longer, but it's the only real path. Cutting corners just gets you burned.

## Stop Dreaming Up Ideas in a Vacuum — Be a Smart Copycat First

We programmers tend to have a bit of that technical pride. Copying feels beneath us — we want to create something original, something that disrupts an industry, changes the world. The ambition is admirable, but reality is harsh. Especially for indie developers with no resources, no network, and pockets cleaner than our faces — trying to pull off a groundbreaking innovation out of the gate has a success rate lower than China's football team making the World Cup.

Think about it. A brand new, never-been-done-before thing means: unvalidated market demand, user habits that need to be cultivated from scratch, and marketing channels that need to be invented. Every step is a minefield. Every step burns money, time, and energy. How many of those blows can our little frames take?

So let go of that "I must be unconventional" obsession. In the early days, especially when your primary goal is making money, being a "smart copycat" is probably the safer and more efficient strategy.

But let me be clear: "copying" doesn't mean pixel-perfect replication. That's stupid copying — it's infringement and you'll be getting lawyer letters. Smart copying means studying products that the market has already validated and that are doing well. Look at them:

1.  What pain points do they solve? (Demand validation)
2.  Who are their target users? (User persona)
3.  How do they reach those users? (Acquisition channels)
4.  How do they make money? (Business model)

Once you've studied these thoroughly, you're standing on the shoulders of giants.

As indie developers, our first goal is to survive and put food on the table. Once we've built up some reserves and resources, then we can chase the stars and the sea, create original stuff, and change the world. At this stage, be pragmatic. Find a good role model, learn from their playbook, and make some money first. Isn't that the smarter move?

## Keeping Your Mindset Steady Is the Most Important Thing

It's only been three months, but I've joined a few indie hacking groups and gotten to know this community. This path — call it freedom at its best, fighting alone without supplies at its worst. You're running on nothing but passion (and instant noodles).

On this road, temptations and blows come in pairs. One day you see some Product Hunt hit blowing up; the next day you scroll past an Indie Hackers post about some bigshot making tens of thousands of dollars a month. Then you look at your own analytics — a handful of lonely users, or maybe zero. the emotional drop is enough to make you question your entire existence.

Or you grind for months, ship your product, and the market response is tepid — barely a ripple. Then the combination punches come: frustration, anxiety, self-doubt. If your mentality is even slightly fragile, you'll just lie down and give up. Or worse, forced by an empty wallet, back to the corporate grind.

Another scenario: you're grinding along and suddenly lose interest, or you spot a new "trend" and your fingers get itchy to pivot. That "grass is always greener" mindset is another indie dev killer. Dabbling left and right ends up with nothing built.

So friends, skills, ideas, and execution are all important. But what will carry you further and more steadily on this road is your mental game. You need a bit of Ah Q spirit and a thick skin.

How do you keep your mindset steady?

1.  **Stop comparing yourself to others**: When people flash their income, treat it like a power fantasy novel. Everyone has their own pace and their own lane. Staring at others only breeds anxiety. Compare yourself to yesterday's you. Progress is a win.
2.  **Embrace uncertainty**: Indie hacking is inherently an adventure. Nobody can promise you success. Enjoy the process of creation. Treat failures as XP packs.
3.  **Break down goals, celebrate small wins**: Don't try to get fat in one bite. Break big goals into small tasks. Every time you finish one, give yourself a pat on the back — or treat yourself to something nice (like an extra egg in your instant noodles).
4.  **Find peers, share warmth**: Find other indie devs, start a group, vent together, share experiences, cheer each other on. You'll find you're not alone in this fight (even if you're all equally broke).
5.  **Rest when you need it**: When you feel your mindset cracking, don't push through. Go for a walk, play some games, watch a movie, step away from the computer. Sharpening the axe won't delay the cutting.
6.  **Going back to a day job isn't shameful**: I used to think indie hacking was my chance to prove myself, and slinking back to a 9-to-5 would be a massive failure. But now I realize it's just a choice. What do you want to pursue? What are you willing to give up? How do you balance and prioritize? These are all choices on your own path. Besides, survive first, then thrive. Nothing shameful about that.

Bottom line: make yourself a cockroach that can't be killed. When you hit a wall, dust yourself off, drop a few choice curse words, and keep moving forward. Steady mindset, and everything becomes possible. Stay strong!
