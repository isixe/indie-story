The year 2025 came to a close with Manus being acquired by Meta. Looking back, it's already my second year without a full-time job, my second year as an indie developer, and my second year running a solopreneur operation.

To be honest, this year hasn't been easy — but it's been the most comfortable, least anxious, and most deeply thoughtful year I've ever had.

From the confusion about AI products at the start of the year, to finding some direction mid-year, to finally committing to my path by year-end — I gradually found my own rhythm and became more confident in the solopreneur path.

## My Three Keywords: Entrepreneurship, Indie Development, Solopreneurship

### Why Choose the Solopreneur Path?

Over the past decade-plus in the internet industry, our understanding of fundraising, hiring, and scaling has been built on the premise of sufficient market dividends — the idea that we can take an idea and execute it to perfection.

But in this AI era, I believe the so-called dividends, and even our cognitive advantages and information asymmetry, are gradually being leveled out.

I think the priority should be to maintain a solopreneur operating model. This choice may seem unlikely to succeed to many, but for me, it's the most suitable decision after careful consideration of the current landscape.

**About entrepreneurship,** for me it's not about creating the next unicorn, but about building a sustainable lifestyle. I don't need 10x growth every year. I need enough to cover living expenses, with time and energy left to explore directions I'm genuinely interested in — like metaphysics.

**Indie development** is the most comfortable working state for me as a programmer. Especially this year, with deep use of AI, I've gained full control over the entire process — from product conception to code implementation, from design to operations. This allows me to iterate quickly and ensure every product is refined according to my vision.

No meetings, no reports, no compromises. This kind of freedom is priceless. That's why, starting in the second half of this year, all product coding work was done by AI.

**Through constant project adjustments and refinements this year, I gradually found my solopreneur rhythm** — the best balance after experiencing life as a cog in the big-tech machine. It's not an escape, but an active choice: I control my work pace, freely choose collaborators I trust, and decide which projects are worth my time. The results this year prove that one person can create valuable products and earn a stable income.

### This Year's Results

Even working solo, the results are nothing to sneeze at — and I also had friends helping me on a part-time basis. Beyond NotesToNotion, I gradually built out the solopreneur system I've established in Notion. While I haven't publicly released this framework yet, I'm confident there will be users who resonate with my philosophy. More importantly, I've used my experience to validate the feasibility of the solopreneur model and found my own business rhythm.

## The First Product Journey: Lessons from NotesToNotion

### The Awakening After Six Months

NotesToNotion was the only product I launched this year. Initially, I thought it could operate with minimal investment. In reality, I poured enormous effort into refining features and optimizing the experience — and I wrote all the code by hand, which was extremely inefficient.

Since integrating Xiaohongshu note forwarding, user growth has been steady, and it genuinely solved the pain point of migrating notes to Notion for some users.

But after six months, I began to realize a harsh truth: **User demand doesn't necessarily mean it's a good product direction.**

What was the problem with this product?

First, **the usage frequency was too low.** Users might only need it when they want to transfer all their notes, and after that, they never need it again. The product has very low presence in users' lives. This makes it difficult to build sustained user engagement and repeat purchases.

Second, **the ceiling was too obvious.** The target user base is limited, and Notion's market is primarily overseas. For an indie developer, this means you'd need to target the overseas note-migration market to have any room to survive.

Finally, **maintenance costs outweighed revenue.** The more notes users transferred to Notion, the higher the costs. Maintaining this migration product required continuous time investment, but the revenue couldn't match it.

### A Difficult But Correct Decision

After facing reality, I made a decision: **going forward, I would only deliver service to paying members.**

This wasn't an easy decision, but as an indie developer, I have to be responsible for my own time and energy. Rather than burning resources on a direction with an obvious ceiling, it's better to invest in areas with more potential.

The biggest lesson from this experience is that **indie developers need to learn to cut their losses.** Not every product is worth sticking with. Sometimes, knowing when to give up is the greatest responsibility you can show yourself. I'll continue providing operational services to users who trusted me and paid, but I won't expand investment in this product anymore.

## Cognitive Awakening: The Pursuit of Small and Beautiful

### Two Books That Changed My Perspective

This year, I read two books that profoundly influenced me: *The Company of One* and *The Almanack of Naval Ravikant*. These books made me re-examine my definition of success.

In middle age, I can no longer obsessively pursue big-tech work experience and titles. Behind those glamorous positions are often endless overtime, repeated inefficient cross-team communication, and the complete sacrifice of personal time. I've seen too many people earn high salaries at big tech companies while losing their health, family, and sense of self.

I've also stopped blindly chasing quick wealth. The stories of getting rich overnight are tempting — like the three founders of Manus — but the probability of success is extremely low, and it often comes at a tremendous cost. More importantly, even if you do make money, what's the point if you've lost your health and time?

That said, for friends who haven't yet had big-tech experience — if you're still young, I still recommend prioritizing big companies. Beyond gaining credentials, the more important reason is to learn from brilliant people, improve your cognitive level, problem analysis skills, and logical thinking abilities.

### Small and Beautiful Is My Answer

*The Company of One* taught me that **growth isn't the only goal — sustainability is.** You don't need to pursue scale, you don't need to manage a team. One person can run a business very well. The key is to find your niche market and provide high-quality products and services.

But I believe a solopreneur doesn't necessarily mean a company of just one person. AI can also be my employee. Through my influence, I can find like-minded friends. Through collaboration models like part-time work and outsourcing, we can accomplish valuable things together.

*The Almanack of Naval Ravikant* made me understand that **true wealth is freedom.** Not the number in your bank account, but the time you can freely control, the ability to choose what you want to do. This kind of freedom is the most valuable asset for a middle-aged person.

So I chose the path of small and beautiful. Not seeking to be big, but seeking to be excellent; not seeking to be fast, but seeking to last. This path may not make me the next billionaire, but it will let me live the life and state I want.

## From Confusion to Clarity: Finding My Own Rhythm

### The Confusion at the Start of the Year

At the beginning of 2025, I was quite lost. The direction for NotesToNotion wasn't clear yet. I had no idea what products to build next or which path to take. Watching other people's products launch one after another, I felt anxious, doubted myself, and even considered giving up indie development altogether.

### The Breakthrough in the Second Half

But by the second half of the year, with a deeper understanding of the market and a clearer sense of self, I gradually found my own indie development path.

**First path: Collaborating with others on products.** I realized that not everything has to be built from scratch. Finding the right partners and leveraging each other's strengths can create better products. This isn't giving up independence — it's being more intelligently independent.

The result: I helped restructure an AR cultural tourism product for my classmate's company — City Trail. From a mini-program to a full-process configuration management backend, to backend API implementation, 90% of the code was done through Vibe Coding.

**Second path: Outputting Notion solopreneur model experience.**

I've been using Notion for 5 years now. Through these two years of practice, I've accumulated considerable experience in using Notion to manage a solopreneur operation. This experience is highly valuable for other indie developers, freelancers, and solopreneurs.

I originally planned to release this at the end of the year, but for various reasons, I haven't officially announced it yet. Keeping some mystery for now — I won't disclose details to avoid copycats.

But in the first half of 2026, I will officially launch these experience collections, including tools, courses, and consulting, to help more indie developers (including solopreneurs) improve their operational efficiency.

**Third path: Creating my own AI products.** AI is the tool I've used most deeply this year. I know its potential well, but it's also the most competitive direction in the current market. I won't make it my core path, because the cost of creating AI products is already approaching zero.

I will continue building truly practical AI tools based on my own needs and pain points. This is a direction that has both market demand and room for continuous iteration. I already have two AI products in the pipeline.

Let me give a sneak peek: one of them targets the gold and silver quantitative trading market, and a beta will be launching soon. If you follow this space, feel free to DM me. The second product is a browser extension — also a browser-side tool — currently on hold. I'll restart it depending on how the AI landscape evolves.

In fact, these three paths don't conflict — they complement each other. Together, they form my business ecosystem, giving me both stable income streams and room for innovation and exploration.

## Health First: The Road to ACL Recovery

### Recovery Is a Marathon

2025 was my first year of recovery after ACL reconstruction surgery. This year, I tried many exercises and rehabilitation routines I'd never done before: strength training, core training, swimming, cycling…

This process gave me a whole new understanding of sports rehabilitation and health. **The human body is a system that needs careful maintenance.** Having surgery isn't the end — the subsequent rehabilitation is just as important, if not more so.

My exercise used to revolve only around soccer. With this new understanding, I discovered more interests, like cycling and core training. Cycling is likely a new direction I'll explore in the future — after all, I was really into riding bikes as a kid.

### Leg Strength Still in Progress

But honestly, since I've been able to exercise again, **my leg strength still hasn't fully recovered.** While daily life is fine, there's still a noticeable gap compared to before the injury. I haven't done enough squats, and I still don't dare try jumping movements — which shows the recovery road is still long.

**In 2026, building leg strength is one of my major goals.** Not to show off muscle, but to be able to move freely, to continue experiencing hiking trips, and to stay active well into middle age and beyond.

This experience also taught me that without health, no amount of wealth or achievement matters. So no matter how busy work gets, I never skip my daily rehab training. I use Notion to set goals, check in, and manage tasks for my recovery routine.

## AI Empowerment: The Secret to 300% Efficiency Gains

### AI Has Completely Changed How I Work

If I had to pick the most impactful tools of 2025, they would be Cursor and ChatGPT. This was my first year of deep AI usage, and it **completely solved many of the difficulties I faced in creation, helping me improve efficiency by 300%.**

This isn't an exaggeration — it's a real feeling. For example:

**Writing Xiaohongshu notes.** It used to take me 2-3 hours to polish a single post. Now with AI assistance, I can finish in 30 minutes with higher quality. AI helps me optimize titles, extract key takeaways, and adjust structure — making my posts more visible and engaging.

**Writing code.** As a programmer, I no longer insist on handwriting everything. AI has at least doubled my coding efficiency. Not just simple code completion, but also bug fixing, code refactoring, technical solution design, and UI mockup implementation. When encountering unfamiliar tech stacks, AI quickly provides examples and explanations, greatly reducing the learning curve. It can even summarize business workflows and solutions based on the project codebase.

**Design output.** While I'm not a professional designer, with AI I can quickly produce high-quality design work. From color schemes to layout suggestions, from icon generation to prototyping, AI provides enormous help. A real example:

Based on my second collaboration project, at my request, all UI design was migrated to Figma. Leveraging Figma's MCP capabilities, I achieved near pixel-perfect reproduction from design mockups to mini-program code — currently approaching 90% fidelity. Each high-fidelity reproduction frees up my hands, letting me relax with tea and casual games.

**Analyzing problems and giving advice.** When I'm confused about product direction or torn on technical choices, AI is my best "consultant." It can analyze problems from multiple angles, offer different solutions, and help me make more informed decisions.

The most real experience: when I'm about to develop a new tool or product, I ask ChatGPT directly — even for advice on how to apply for a valuable domain name. ChatGPT provides excellent suggestions.

### Advice for 2026: Embrace AI

**I strongly urge any friend reading my blog to embrace AI in 2026 and make using AI a top priority.**

Don't treat AI as optional — treat it as essential. Just like learning to use search engines back in the day, not knowing how to use AI now means voluntarily giving up your competitive edge.

Let me be clear: **AI is a tool, not a replacement.** It can't replace your thinking or your creativity, but it can make your thinking deeper and help your creativity land better.

Learning how to ask questions, how to guide, how to leverage AI's output — these are essential skills for everyone in 2026.
