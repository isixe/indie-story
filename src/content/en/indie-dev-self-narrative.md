## Foreword

Hi everyone, I'm an indie developer. To be more specific, I'm an indie developer who has been building web applications for three years.

When many people talk about indie developers, the first thing that comes to mind is: Do they make money? Can they support themselves? What are their income sources?

In short, when it comes to indie developers, it always boils down to one word: "money."

So let me share my mental journey over these years as an indie developer — consider it a summary of my experience. Hopefully, it can also offer some insights to those who want to become indie developers or are already on this path.

**Indie development website:** [91Huajian](https://91huajian.cn/)

## 1. The Budding Stage

I majored in a computer-related field in college. By "related," I mean it was somewhat connected to computer science but not really core CS. As a result, my programming skills were quite weak throughout college.

Around my junior year, I kept seeing various software, apps, and websites online, and I really wanted to build something of my own. Back then, it wasn't about money — I simply wanted to learn technology and turn my wild ideas into something real.

But due to my lack of technical skills and a fair amount of laziness, I gave up almost as soon as I started.

Eventually, I chose an easier path to begin with: **starting a WeChat Official Account.**

I had already started learning front-end development, so I initially shared front-end knowledge on the account. I stuck with it for a while, but the readership was abysmal, and I soon shelved it.

By the time I graduated, I still hadn't built my own application. I was just learning front-end skills on and off. The only thing I'd maintained intermittently was that WeChat Official Account.

## 2. Building a Personal Blog (First Year After Graduation)

After graduating, my front-end skills weren't exceptional, but I managed to land a front-end development job. And so began my journey as a programmer.

In my first year on the job, I threw myself into work. Although the idea of "building my own application" was still in the back of my mind, it kept getting postponed.

Later, when work became less hectic, I started working on setting up a personal blog. I kept seeing this saying: **A good programmer should have their own independent blog.**

I wanted a little corner of my own too — a place to document my life and technical growth. So I started building a personal blog. Sticking to the free route, I used GitHub to set up a free personal blog.

Later on, I even wrote an article about how to use GitHub to build a free personal blog — but that was several years ago.

![](/content/indie-dev-self-narrative/blog-github-pages.png)

After running the blog for a while, I felt the access speed was too slow. So I bought my own server and domain name, planning to build one from scratch.

Getting the domain name filed (ICP filing) took quite a while, since domestic regulations in China don't allow filing a personal blog under the blog category.

Eventually, I used an open-source blog framework and finally deployed the blog on my own server. Then I started writing articles and also cross-published them on my WeChat Official Account.

But after a few months, I gave up. Looking back, the main reasons were:

1.  **The server subscription expired, and renewal was too expensive.**
2.  **I had too many ideas — I kept wanting to tweak the blog's features and design, but frameworks were too limiting for customization.**
3.  **Writing and maintaining an independent blog was too much hassle. I wanted to focus on one thing.**
4.  **Nobody was reading my articles — no motivation.**

## 3. Going All-in on the WeChat Official Account (Second Year After Graduation)

After giving up on the personal blog, I started thinking about a new direction. I ultimately settled on the WeChat Official Account — because, at that point, it was the only thing I was still maintaining.

But my earlier account mainly shared front-end knowledge and articles, which had too narrow an audience. By then, I'd been working for two years and wasn't as naive anymore. I was thinking about making money, wondering if I could turn the account into a side hustle.

So I renamed the account and changed the content direction to focus on sharing various websites, black-tech tools, software, and the like.

That year, every day after work, the first thing I did was prepare the next day's article, schedule it, and have it sent out early the next morning.

After a year of hard work, the account's follower count gradually grew — from just over 10 followers, to 100, to qualifying for ad monetization (Traffic Master), then 1,000, 5,000, and eventually 10,000 followers.

During that time, I also developed a mini-program (WeChat Mini Program), which reached around 10,000 users.

But mini-programs are really unfriendly to individual developers. Most features I worked hard to build would get permanently banned within a couple of days. I had no choice but to give up on personal mini-programs.

![](/content/indie-dev-self-narrative/wechat-platform-income.png)

That year I barely rested. I checked my account every day and kept publishing articles.

Once I hit the follower threshold, Traffic Master (WeChat's ad program) started generating a little income, and I could also pick up some ads on various platforms.

But overall, the income was really low. To date, my total Traffic Master earnings are only around a thousand RMB, plus ad revenue — maybe two or three thousand RMB total.

Long story short: it's nothing like the "make 10K a month" or "passive income" hype you see online.

![](/content/indie-dev-self-narrative/traffic-master-income.png)

After more than a year of effort with very little return, I decided to pause investing further in this direction. The input was too high and the results were diminishing.

## 4. Writing Articles and Recording Videos (Third Year After Graduation)

From the third year onward, I updated the WeChat account only sporadically, without putting much energy into it.

But I can't sit still. My ambitions were never fulfilled. I always felt my dream was still alive, that there was still a chance. So I started a new direction.

After three years of work, my front-end skills had improved significantly. I decided to start writing front-end articles and recording related videos.

But this time, I didn't build another personal blog. Instead, I submitted articles directly to various platforms and focused purely on writing technical content.

So I started cranking out content on three platforms: [Zhihu](https://www.zhihu.com/people/luhongquan), [Juejin](https://juejin.cn/user/3034307822112798), and [Bilibili](https://space.bilibili.com/493520625?spm_id_from=333.1007.0.0)

At my peak, I published articles daily for a whole month without missing a day. Every article was written from scratch after coming home from work.

Looking back, I was really passionate back then. After finishing an article each night, I'd also record a tutorial video for it.

I kept this up on and off for about a year, writing maybe one to two hundred articles.

Although I didn't make any real money that year, it felt incredibly fulfilling. I also gained some followers.

![](/content/indie-dev-self-narrative/zhihu-profile.png)
![](/content/indie-dev-self-narrative/juejin-profile.png)
![](/content/indie-dev-self-narrative/bilibili-profile.png)

Juejin was the platform that motivated me the most back then. Every time I participated in their article-writing events, I'd win some nice prizes, which made me really happy.

![](/content/indie-dev-self-narrative/juejin-prizes.png)

After a year of effort, I gained a lot: followers, knowledge, and a stronger will.

But it still wasn't quite what I had in mind. What I really wanted was my own application — a meaningful side hustle.

## 5. Building a Web Navigation Site (End of Third Year After Graduation)

After the trials of the previous years, I felt my technical skills had improved significantly. I was now capable of building a complete application on my own.

So I started thinking about what to build. Naturally, I focused on the web, since that's what I'd been doing professionally.

I settled on a relatively simple project: a web navigation site.

Why this project? Here were my main considerations:

1.  A web navigation site is easy to build with simple features
2.  My WeChat Official Account overlapped with this kind of project
3.  I personally had a need to collect and organize websites

Once I decided on the project, I started development, while also buying a server, registering a domain, and going through the ICP filing process.

The development cycle was short, but it was still painful.

At work, whenever I had a free moment, I'd work on the navigation site. After getting home, I'd keep working on it.

For a month or two, I barely rested. The main reason was that I kept running into problems and new technologies that I had to learn and overcome one by one.

After months of development and preparation, the site finally launched. I called it "SmllPig Navigation" (小猪导航).

Until a couple of months ago, my current product — 91Huajian — was still using the domain `smllpig.site` as a file server. That domain has now been completely decommissioned. Since there's no active site on it, the ICP filing couldn't pass, so I had to cancel it.

After SmllPig Navigation launched, it still got a few hundred visits a day, and it was indexed by Baidu. But in the end, I abandoned the project. Here's why:

1.  I started it as a practice project — mainly to figure out the technology and development process.
2.  A navigation site is hard to monetize; it's tough to even get ads.
3.  I had a new idea.

## 6. 91Huajian

[91Huajian](https://91huajian.cn/) is the project I'm currently running, and it's been under continuous maintenance since the end of 2022.

By the end of 2022, the navigation site was mostly stable, so I started this new project.

This project is more complex than the previous ones. I wanted it to be a more complete project with a modern tech stack.

Let me briefly introduce it:

> 91Huajian is an open-source, free resume design and creation platform that also offers template downloads. The front-end is fully open-source and free.
> The site includes a complete admin dashboard for easy site management.
> It features two built-in design tools — quick design, resume, cover, and poster creation — all free to use, with one-click export to high-resolution PDF, JSON data, and more.
> The platform offers various free ways to earn credits, making resource downloads and exports completely free.

The front-end has always been open-source and free, and it's been well received. The GitHub repo has over 1.5k stars.

This is my favorite project so far. I've poured a lot of heart into it, and it's the project that made me truly feel like an indie developer.

If you're interested, go ahead and try it out. I'm open to any suggestions or feedback:

[91Huajian — A Free, Open-Source Resume Building Tool](https://91huajian.cn/)

## 7. The Indie Developer Path

Looking back at the past five years, it feels like I've never really taken a break. It's not that I don't want to rest, nor that I love working that much.

On the contrary, I constantly wish I could fully relax and get some proper rest.

But I can't — simply because the dream inside me has never been realized.

Some people ask me: In five years, with all that you've done, how much money have you made?

Let me be honest: not much. The returns over these years are far from matching the effort I've put in.

But I don't regret it. My direction is gradually becoming clearer. Finding something you truly love in this life is not easy.

As of today, I can proudly call myself an indie developer — not because I've made a lot of money from my app, but because my projects have given my life more meaning.

I often see articles and posts online about indie developers. People love to give advice — telling you what to build, what field to enter, what kind of software to avoid, and so on.

But all I want to say is: if you want to become an indie developer, you must master these two words:

**Keep going!**

And I will keep going too:

-   Keep updating and maintaining my project
-   Keep learning about website operations
-   Stay true to my original intention
-   Keep this perseverance alive

Finally, if you'd like to connect with me, feel free to add me or follow me on my social platforms.
