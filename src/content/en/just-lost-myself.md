The saying goes, "Youth is never without confusion." But for me, my youth was never confused -- when I wanted to play, I played freely; when I wanted to get things done, I got them done. Yet now, in this post-youth era (I'm not even sure if I can still call it youth), confusion has begun to creep in.

It all started this week when I realized something: I seem to be constantly busy. There's plenty of work, but if I think carefully, aside from work, I can't really say what I'm busy with, let alone what truly matters.

## Stuck in Place

From time to time, I feel like I still have so many shortcomings:

- My English is still poor -- I can't hold a fluent conversation
- I'm not a programming master either -- I don't know much about low-level computer principles
- I'm not good at social relationships, nor at building a network (something people at my career stage probably should be doing)
- My weight keeps going up, but fitness and dieting never quite start
- I lack soft skills at work -- I'm not good at presenting or packaging my accomplishments... *no wait* -- at reporting achievements or reading organizational dynamics
- And many other things

Actually, these issues themselves don't bother me. With my half-baked abilities, I get by well enough in daily work and life. I've always believed in looking at things dynamically -- temporary struggles aren't really struggles if you zoom out.

What truly bothers me is this: I seem to be standing still on all these fronts. None of these problems are new discoveries -- I've known about them for a long time. So why has nothing changed?

The pattern is always the same: study English for a couple of days, then feel soft skills are lacking, so read a bit on soft skills; a few days later, feel the need to level up programming; then dive into AI, start a new project, and the cycle repeats.

## What Drives Me

The real issue seems to be: I don't have a backbone. I'm driven more by problems than by goals. Or by passion? By ideals?

I've fallen into a "good enough" mentality -- problem-driven: when something goes wrong, I patch it up until it's good enough. Done. Then what? Wait for the next problem to show up, and patch that one too.

So it's not that I decide what I want to do -- it's that things and problems push me forward. Once a problem is solved, the driving force disappears.

Objectively speaking, work does consume most of my energy. But if I really squeezed, I could carve out some time for myself. And I have -- but more often than not, I spend it on inertia: following tech trends, reading industry news, staying informed. A small portion goes into actively building projects or experimenting with new things.

But when I think about it, this feels like a low-quality allocation of time. I'm putting in time and energy -- but toward what goal? It circles back to the question: what drives me? If I don't even have a goal, what's the point of all this?

People often say, "Just do good deeds and don't worry about the future." But "doing good deeds" presupposes that you've defined what a "good deed" is. I even start to doubt: at this stage of my life, is what I'm doing even a good deed?

## Maturity or Fading Away

In some ways, I feel like I'm becoming more mature.

I've gotten better at controlling my emotions. In fact, a lot of the time, I don't have many emotions at all. I've also become less willing to express myself.

I no longer think things are cool for the sake of being cool, nor do I do things without purpose -- like suddenly deciding to bike for hours to some distant place.

I don't do outrageous things anymore. Like walking with a friend and suddenly saying, "Should we just scream in the street?" and then doing it before they can answer, without caring about the stares.

I no longer feel like a fresh graduate. More than that -- I feel like I'm no longer that young person fresh out of school. Some things are for them to do. I've aged out of that phase.

I no longer have a clear sense of where I'm going. School days were simple -- the goal was already set, just walk toward it.

I no longer feel curious about the unknown. I used to flash ROMs, explore unfamiliar places, dive into new domains. Now I only seem to care about programming, AI, making money, career development, investing. My focus is still mostly on programming; the rest just feel like things I should care about at this stage, or things that might be useful later.

I can no longer shout slogans. I used to post on social media: "Life is not just about the here and now -- there's poetry and distant fields."

I don't know if this is maturity, or fading away.

## Interesting Matters More Than Meaningful

I once wrote: interesting matters more than meaningful. But lately, I can't seem to find what that "interesting" thing is anymore.

My current state isn't bad. In many ways, I'm in a state of "small certain happiness." But here's the thing about me: at every stage, it seems I need a spark -- something that ignites passion, gives me a strong inner drive, a burning feeling. But right now, that fire has gone out.

So sometimes I envy people who find joy in their own pursuits -- researchers in their fields, or those who can clearly follow their plans step by step. I too wish I could figure out what that "interesting" thing is for me. This article is really just an attempt to sort through my recent thoughts, to understand myself better.

Writing this, I've pretty much covered everything on my mind. But it seems I still haven't found an answer. I need to think more.

Of course, maybe it's as the saying goes: overthinking is the problem, action is the answer. Perhaps some questions can only be understood through doing. When things become clearer, I'll share again.
