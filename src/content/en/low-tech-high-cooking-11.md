Professor Dong Yugan, who works in landscape architecture and building design, made a pointed observation after studying famous homes in Europe and America: furniture is often already factored into the architectural sketches, and decades after the architect has passed away, those pieces of furniture remain exactly where they were — as if no one dares to move them. Furniture becomes architecture, immovable architecture.

Furniture-as-architecture has its pros and cons. On the good side, a Tiffany floor lamp in Steve Jobs's mansion feels like it naturally grew there. On the bad side, think of all those kitchen electrical appliances — especially built-in ones — that try to force an American lifestyle on you (or perhaps the half-Chinese, half-Western mishmash that's trendy now).[^1] In a commercial context, furniture-as-architecture is mostly just a way for capital to extract profit. In the kitchen especially, once you start planning custom cabinets or a built-in refrigerator, you're already on the path of kitchen-prop-as-architecture: a grand gamble where you bet your cooking habits won't change for years — or even a lifetime.[^2]

There must be other ways to design a kitchen, and every home cook has a chance to try them. For example, the [prefabricated houses](https://www.prefabmuseum.uk/content/history/history) built in Britain after WWII featured kitchens that were essentially a lineup of equipment — a roughly uniform set of sinks, stoves, ovens, countertops, and cabinets of equal height and depth. This Lego-like approach, where major kitchen components can be swapped based on the user's habits, deserves to step out of the museum.

Let's start with the most basic yet representative kitchen appliance: the water dispenser. You can live without an oven (especially in a Chinese kitchen), you can skip a microwave (I was too lazy to unpack mine after moving), or you might never cook at all — but you can't go without drinking water. Of course, equating drinking water with a water dispenser is already stepping into the trap of furniture-as-architecture.

The problem with water dispensers is that they usually need electricity, which means they must sit near an outlet; they're either top-heavy with a huge jug perched on top, or tethered to a water line (or a floor bucket)… In the end, the water dispenser becomes something that can theoretically be moved, but practically no one wants to. It becomes architecture. Many large kitchen appliances go through the same process.

When I moved last time, a friend gave me a small water dispenser with a heating function. I tried using it for a while and soon realized that no matter how they look on the outside, they're just as filthy as the ones that used to sit in classroom corners: water sits trapped in the drip tray and tubes, impossible to clean unless you take it apart. I suspect you'd need to own two or three dispensers, swapping them out like you change underwear, so you have backups while the others dry — otherwise, you're drinking from a bacteria petri dish. My elementary school was unusually serious about hygiene — they really did wash water dispensers like underwear.

Needless to say, I know what the classic solution is: invest in a fancier dispenser — one that filters, or heats, or both if you're really worried. I roughly did the math and immediately concluded that even buying bottled water every day for cooking would be cheaper than messing around with water purifiers or boiling units. Of course, I'm not about to stockpile hundreds of bottles of water at home like I'm preparing for the apocalypse.

Even if you somehow solve all these problems, the best you get is a wall-mounted boiling unit — and then you find yourself sprinting to grab water just as your soup pot is about to burn dry (you mentioned an under-sink water purifier? Let's not pile on more tech debt…). At the end of the day, most of the problems with water dispensers come from the furniture-as-architecture mindset (plus that peculiar national sentiment that "Chinese people must drink hot water" — who knows if it's just a marketing gimmick cooked up by foreign capital).

And that's where the low-tech approach comes in. In many temporary office spaces and construction sites, the procurement team chooses simple bucket stands instead of plugged-in, plumbed[^3] water dispensers to save costs. These solutions are straightforward: replace the bucket cap with a faucet, flip the bucket upside down, and gravity does the work — giving you running water without electricity or plumbing.

![](/content/low-tech-high-cooking-11/bucket-stand.jpeg)

*Low-tech water dispenser: a bucket, a stand, plus a detachable faucet*

These stands are perfect for a kitchen. As long as you can clear a small corner, you get countertop water access — no bending over, no walking far, no need to worry about power outlets or extension cords (which could be deadly in a wet kitchen). Normally, you can leave it in a dim corner as an unplugged water dispenser. When I'm in the middle of fast-paced Chinese stir-frying, I move the whole stand with the bucket over to the stove — I can flip things in the wok with one hand and reach for water with the other (and easily move it back when I'm done).

The whole stand costs only twenty or thirty yuan. If you're especially concerned about hygiene, you can buy 5-liter or smaller buckets to avoid leaving water sitting too long after opening. The stand itself doesn't touch the water; the only part that could grow bacteria is the faucet. You can buy several spares (a few yuan each) and swap them out like underwear. The downside, of course, is that you can't get hot water instantly — but whether the convenience of a traditional dispenser is worth the cost and health risks is debatable. For readers who really want hot water, you could look into the tea-bucket dispensers used in food service — essentially revisiting the old way of boiling a pot of water and drinking it through the day.

To be honest, when I first started looking into water dispensers and alternatives,[^4] I wasn't on some noble mission to dismantle furniture-as-architecture. I just couldn't stand the nitpicky annoyances of conventional dispensers. Only later did I realize the underlying architectural assumption — it forces you into a commitment that spans far too long, too early. The real reason the bucket stand isn't widely adopted isn't the tired cliché of "Chinese people need hot water." It's that it looks temporary, crude, even cheap — more suited for a construction site than a kitchen.

But who knew that the kitchen you imagine is just a conspiracy cooked up by manufacturers? Only your life is yours. If you need to, you can move anything into your kitchen. If you're renovating, don't forget you're the client — you can tell those designers who can barely boil instant noodles to shut up or get lost. If you're renting, you don't have to go all out from the start — and that's a perfect opportunity for a life experiment.

Readers who have one at home, try pulling out the tube and feeling it — see if early-stage biological evolution has begun on it.

Against the backdrop of furniture-as-architecture, no new product is enough to shake this article.
