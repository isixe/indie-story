> Kitchen gadgets have become a massive market. Even peeling a clove of garlic[^1] has over a dozen wildly different designs. I don't really mind how much money manufacturers take from my wallet — the problem is that these novel tools often haven't stood the test of the market. Most of the time, they're just the product of a product manager's whim (every year when major design awards are announced, you see hordes of thoughtless designers cropping up). Celebrity chef Alton Brown [tested a batch of kitchen gadgets](https://www.dailydot.com/irl/alton-brown-gadget-review/), and Chinese chef Gao Han did a [similar review](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Q1013ZcVFY) — both nearly lost their minds.

> The popularity of these gadgets shows two things: the problem they try to solve is real, but it also reflects that modern young people rarely have time to cook. With fewer testing opportunities, it's hard to spot the fragile designs that only reveal themselves through long-term use.

> Admittedly, the modern kitchen — typically a solo young adult (or middle-aged person) cooking for one every now and then, occasionally entertaining friends — might be a fast-growing market where bad designs iterate faster than death. But we can borrow from other mature fields and repurpose proven products.[^2] This article continues the discussion from [*Breaking Down Doors Wearing Out Shoes: How to Find Tools and Domain Principles*](https://utgd.net/article/20711/), and tries to add a few great tools to the kitchen.

## The Best Animal Fat Container: Medical Alcohol Cotton Jar

Keywords: ointment jar, cotton ball jar, disinfectant jar

This article was inspired by a ten-yuan jar. When I cook, I often end up with extra rendered lard. Used in moderation, animal fat is an excellent cooking ingredient (think stir-fried greens in lard — forget chicken breast). So I wanted a lard jar. But the products on the market typically follow traditional designs — big enamel jars or clay pots that remind me of multi-generational family meals from childhood. That kind of jar clearly doesn't suit a modern small apartment; I'd probably take a year to finish a whole jar of lard.

When buying kitchen gear, my instinct is to look for Japanese products first, then search for affordable alternatives. The Japanese are practically neurotic about kitchen tools, and sure enough, many have designed small lard jars (even MUJI made one), but the prices easily hit hundreds. Let's see how these sellers market their products: heat-resistant, enamel anti-rust, airtight lid, keeps your lard fresh and fragrant.

— OK, got it. I need an enamel jar with some sealing ability. I quickly thought of the alcohol cotton jars commonly seen in hospitals. They're also enamel — heat-resistant enough that hot lard won't burn through (so plastic is out), rust-resistant, and with a tight-fitting lid that provides decent sealing (to slow alcohol evaporation).

## The Best Small Storage Bottles: Medical Bottles and Jars

Keywords: reagent bottles, wide-mouth bottles, wash bottles, cryovials… I recommend going straight for Nalgene or Thermo Fisher medical-grade products.

The medical field is a treasure trove. After all, hospitals and labs have hygiene standards that far exceed most kitchens (which are actually dirtier than you think). In [a previous article](https://utgd.net/article/20309/), I introduced some delightful medical storage bottles that I now use instead of fancy travel-sized containers. In the kitchen, these bottles are just as versatile. And the various reagent bottles commonly used in labs are also excellent and inexpensive.

I've noticed that more and more online stores are starting to use these medical containers. Besides being cheap and functional, they look like props straight out of *Breaking Bad* — with built-in atmosphere. Even I, a coffee illiterate, have seen cafes selling coffee beans packed in medicine bottles more than once.

Some shops seem to be making money off information asymmetry. For example, stores with a Japanese minimalist aesthetic sell frosted-glass reagent bottles for hundreds of yuan per set, when the best domestic equivalents cost just a few yuan each with more size options. The only catch is a larger minimum order quantity — I still have a dozen unused bottles at home.

## A Great Salt Cellar Alternative: Iodine Tincture Jar

Keywords: iodine tincture jar, semi-open disinfectant jar

For seasonings you use frequently — salt, sugar, or MSG (whether you like it or not, I don't use it) — an airtight sealed jar isn't ideal. A jar with a small opening is better, so you can dip in a spoon whenever you need.

While hunting for a lard jar, I also looked at containers for other seasonings. Taobao, perhaps sensing my coveting of overpriced Japanese goods, doubled down and recommended high-end European-imported salt cellars. I genuinely liked them, but I found an alternative in the medical supplies section: stainless steel open-top jars for iodine cotton balls. Comparing the prices, there was a two-order-of-magnitude difference.

![](/content/low-tech-high-cooking-6/price-comparison.png)

*High-end product vs. affordable medical supply*

Medical iodine jars also have a lid with an opening — originally designed for inserting tweezers or long cotton swabs, but in the kitchen it works perfectly for a small measuring spoon. The key is that the lid snaps tightly onto the jar, and a hinged half can be flipped open without removing the whole lid. A simple finger flick is all it takes, so there's no loose lid to deal with. I've seen similar designs in restaurant kitchens — clearly, only high-intensity work environments push manufacturers to think about these details.

The cost of one imported jar buys several rows of stainless steel open-top jars. I'm not sure whether a several-times price difference translates to even a modest improvement in user experience.

## The Best Measuring Spoons: Lab Spatulas

Keywords: lab spoon, spatula, powder scoop, micro spoon, reagent spoon

With all those jars, you naturally need a spoon to measure out portions. Unfortunately, the measuring spoons I liked all came in sets, but I only needed the 1g and 5g sizes[^3]. The rest would go unused. And since I have many different seasonings, buying a whole set would leave me with dozens of idle spoons.

After going in circles, I finally found the perfect spoon in a lab supplies store: the lab spatula.

![](/content/low-tech-high-cooking-6/lab-spatula-in-jars.jpeg)

*I put a lab spatula in every seasoning jar*

These spoons come in many sizes, are cheap, and aren't bundled into sets. Made thin as a whisper to reduce costs in industrial applications, they happen to slide effortlessly into seasoning jars — clean and precise.

Unsurprisingly, when it comes to measurement, labs are far ahead of kitchens. In America, it took until about a hundred years ago for kitchens to undergo a measuring revolution — introducing standardized cups, tablespoons, and teaspoons, moving beyond "a pinch" and "a dash." But plenty of kitchen tools are still just for show — walnut or ceramic measuring spoons that look cute and round, yet end up with more seasoning stuck to their surface than actually measured, rendering them completely useless. Lab supplies, on the other hand, had every detail thoroughly considered long ago (recall middle school science class). Bringing them into the kitchen is a total mismatch in sophistication.[^4]

## The Best Spice Grinder: Hand Coffee Grinder

Keywords: hand coffee grinder, coffee mill

Pepper is the most annoying thing in the kitchen. Every time I'm about to use it, it's already clumped, stale, and flavorless. Many readers who value quality of life probably own a pepper grinder for fresh grinding.

Similarly, I don't buy pre-ground cinnamon or nutmeg powder either — before I've used even a tenth, they've turned into sawdust. But a pepper grinder isn't suited for extremely hard spices like cinnamon sticks or whole nutmeg. I once considered buying an herb grinder, but then I thought it through — that thing produces a big batch at once. With small quantities, the powder gets stuck in the machine's crevices, no different from buying pre-ground powder and letting it expire.

Let's think differently: is there a tool that can grind hard plant matter but in small doses? Yes — a hand coffee grinder works perfectly. For grinding, a coffee grinder has an extra-long crank arm. Compared to a pepper mill with a tiny two- or three-centimeter radius, the torque is an order of magnitude greater — way less effort. As for output, a normal person never grinds more than 30g of beans per session (that's already a double shot), so a coffee grinder won't produce a whole vat like an herb grinder would. Meanwhile, the coffee industry has been around for so long that hand grinder designs are mature and affordable. I bought a basic metal one for under ¥100; a nicer one with a ceramic burr runs about ¥100.

![](/content/low-tech-high-cooking-6/hand-grinder-spices.jpeg)

*I often use a hand coffee grinder for spices*

After grinding spices, you can toss in a bit of rice and grind that too — the rice absorbs the spice aroma, so the next time you grind a different spice, there's less flavor transfer.

## The All-Purpose Steam Tongs: Water Pump Pliers

Keywords: water pump pliers, pliers wrench

For a while, I was obsessed with steamed dishes. I desperately needed a plate gripper to avoid burning my hands. Besides regular lifting handles, I wanted a plier-style tong for retrieving oddly angled dishes. It just so happens [I have a pair of water pump pliers at home](https://utgd.net/article/20947/) — they work even better than kitchen-specific gadgets.

Water pump pliers are designed for nuts and steel pipes — way sturdier than any kitchen tool. The jaw opening is adjustable, handling everything from thin baking sheets to tagine pot lids (an extra-thick clay pot). If you're worried about the jaws damaging your cookware's coating, just grab a paper towel to wrap around them. Meanwhile, those dedicated steam tongs are too rigid and basically useless for anything else.

![](/content/low-tech-high-cooking-6/water-pump-pliers.jpeg)

*I always keep a pair of water pump pliers in my kitchen toolkit*

I have several pairs of these pipe pliers at home. Besides handling hot dishes in the kitchen, they're also great for tightening water heater valves, adjusting bicycle axles, pulling out white hairs, or fixing toilet seat lids (though I wouldn't recommend using the cooking pair for that). Many specialized kitchen tools — especially novel ones — have more versatile, more mature, and more durable alternatives.

You can pick rust-resistant coated versions. The ones I have happen to be uncoated and rust-prone, but they work well enough. I've gotten into the habit of drying all my kitchen tools by hand anyway, rather than blindly trusting stainless steel.

## The Searing Secret Weapon: Medical Forceps

Keywords: medical forceps, lab tweezers

When I first started learning Western cooking, I followed the trend and bought barbecue tongs — supposedly perfect for flipping meat chunks, steaks, or chicken thighs. I got the classic design by Japanese designer Naoto Fukasawa, whose knockoffs are now seen in almost every malatang shop. Fukasawa's tongs clearly come from the era of American backyard barbecues — thick, wide-mouthed, as if they want to flip a three-pound tomahawk steak in one go. This makes them terrible for turning smaller pieces of meat. Sure enough, someone smelled an opportunity and created what they call a "[modernist barbecue tong](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r7Pb6sxQ0jg)", which looks like someone took a normal pair of tongs and scaled only the width in Photoshop.

Instead of blindly testing new shapes, why not try a more proven clamping tool? Large medical forceps are right there, ready to go. A designer's brainchild might not even grip a piece of meat properly — it'll keep slipping[^5] — while medical forceps that have handled countless血肉 (flesh and blood) in the operating room find gripping meat and vegetables to be child's play.

Some Korean dramas have already shown forceps being used in kitchens and even at the dining table. Serious Eats also recommends keeping a few pairs of forceps in the kitchen (for both cooking and plating). Given that these things remind people of clinical settings, domestic readers might need some time to get used to the idea.

## Summary

By following domain principles, we can find excellent alternatives to many novel kitchen gadgets. More often than not, those "new inventions" turn out to be pure stupidity taxes.

That said, the main goal of this article isn't to teach you how to save money. Some of the tools mentioned here aren't cheap (water pump pliers, for example).

Also, many products that are already well-established in the kitchen world don't need reinventing. Carbon steel knives that have been used for a thousand years, wooden cutting boards, ceramic vessels — they're probably still the best choice. Any design that lasts a millennium is its own best endorsement.

Perhaps someday, when I find myself lost in an unfamiliar field without the right tools, I'll look back at the pots and pans in my kitchen and find inspiration there.
