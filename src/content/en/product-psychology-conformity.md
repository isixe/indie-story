> Most people, in most situations, are easily influenced by the behavior of those around them, and tend to align their perceptions, judgments, and opinions with the majority or public consensus. Businesses have long exploited this psychology to promote their products.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/cover.jpg)

If you step into an elevator and everyone is facing away from the door, would you go along with them? You might not think so, but psychologists have found that many people do exactly that.

Psychologists once conducted an interesting experiment. They had several confederates board an elevator and all face away from the door. When real unsuspecting riders entered and saw this scene, they were visibly confused. Though they initially stood facing the right direction, it didn't take long before they began to follow the crowd and turn around to face the same way as everyone else.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/elevator-experiment.jpg)

Behind this behavior is the conformity effect, working quietly and unnoticed.

## So, What Is Conformity?

Conformity is **the tendency of people to align with the group — to do what others are doing**. Generally, people conform for two main reasons:

- **Informational influence**: When faced with unfamiliar situations, people look to others' behavior as a guide to avoid making mistakes.
- **Normative influence**: Every group has its own unwritten norms. To fit in and gain acceptance, people tend to conform to the group as much as possible.

### Asch's Conformity Experiment

Psychologist Solomon E. Asch designed an experiment with a group of seven people, six of whom were actors and only one an actual unknowing participant.

The seven sat in a semicircle. The experimenter showed them a card with a standard line on one side and three lines of different lengths on the other. Each person was asked to say which line matched the standard line in length.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/asch-experiment.png)

The six actors deliberately gave the same wrong answer one after another. The unknowing participant, within just a few rounds, began to doubt their own judgment and went along with the group's incorrect answer.

Asch repeated this experiment many times. The results showed that about three-quarters of participants conformed at least once, and 37% of all answers given by participants were incorrect — yielding to the group's pressure.

When interviewed afterward, participants reported feeling significant psychological pressure and internal conflict during the experiment. Asch himself was shocked by the results. He said: **"The tendency to conformity in our society is so strong that reasonably intelligent and well-meaning young people are willing to call white black."** This shows just how powerful the conformity effect can be.

## Applications of Conformity

In psychology, the conformity effect is both important and widely recognized. As long as we can get a few people to serve as role models, we can influence the behavior of others.

Many businesses and individuals consciously or unconsciously apply this principle, and we can find examples everywhere in our daily lives.

Here are a few simple examples:

(1) At donation sites, if we see money already in the box, we're more likely to contribute. Conversely, if the box is empty, we're less likely to put money in. Many fundraisers pre-seed the box with some cash to trigger people's tendency to conform.

(2) Some e-commerce companies display purchase data prominently throughout their product pages, encouraging visitors to follow the crowd and buy.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/purchase-data.jpg)

(3) Even without using numbers, many product advertisements create the impression of mass adoption through imagery alone — suggesting popularity. Beverage ads often use this technique.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/drink-ad.jpg)

## Factors Influencing Conformity

### 1. Group Size

Group size affects the conformity effect. Experiments show that conformity rates increase as the group grows — three people are significantly more influential than one. However, once the group exceeds five people, the conformity increase levels off. **To use conformity to win someone over, five people demonstrating the behavior is generally sufficient.**

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/group-size.jpg)

Similarly, when building a conformity environment for marketing, we should aim to provide at least five examples. When some education products feature student testimonials on their landing pages, they typically include five or more — which is a very appropriate approach.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/testimonials.jpg)

### 2. Consistency

In Asch's conformity experiments, although everyone unanimously gave misleading answers, if just one person spoke up with the correct answer, the participant would follow suit.

This result tells us that **as long as one person shares your position, resisting group pressure becomes much easier**. Even if the dissenter's view is wrong, it strengthens other individuals' independence and breaks the conformity cycle.

Many Meituan merchants try to eliminate negative reviews — for instance, by calling the customer to settle privately and asking them to delete the review. The goal is to maintain consistency in the reviews. Conversely, if you leave negative reviews unaddressed, it's highly likely that many potential customers will be scared off.

### 3. Cohesion

The pressure to conform from people outside the group is far weaker than from those inside the group. **You are far more likely to be influenced by colleagues and friends than by unfamiliar strangers.**

The stronger the cohesion of a group, the greater its influence on its members.

For example, monks in a Buddhist temple are more likely to be influenced by their own master and senior brothers than by Taoist priests in a Taoist temple — this is cohesion at work.

The referral marketing tactics commonly used by businesses work precisely because the recommendations come from friends — people within one's social circle — making it easier for customers to be influenced by group cohesion and purchase.

This principle also reminds us that we should always find ways to encourage customers to recommend products to each other.

### 4. Status

Within a group, **those with higher status tend to have greater influence, and lower-status members are more likely to defer to them**.

For example, when it comes to jaywalking, if the person doing it is well-dressed and appears to be of high social status, their demonstration effect is stronger — more people will follow them across the street.

Many companies feature their high-end clients on their promotional materials, leveraging this status-based influence. The endorsement of certain authoritative clients can be ten times more effective than an ordinary one.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/status-example.jpg)

Many book covers list the names of authoritative figures who recommend the book — this too is a use of status to create a conformity impulse.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/book-endorsements.jpg)

### 5. Public Response

**People are more likely to conform in public than in private.** In Asch's conformity studies, when participants were asked to write down their answers instead of stating them aloud, they were less influenced by group pressure.

In public settings, people care more about their image and are more careful to avoid standing out.

When users shop online, most decisions are made privately. **If you can show them what other users are doing, while also making their own actions visible to others**, people will naturally experience the effect of public pressure.

Group buying is a good example of this public effect in action. As a user, you can see others' participation records. At the same time, your own participation is made public. Especially when this information circulates in WeChat groups, the feeling of public exposure intensifies, and the conformity effect becomes even stronger.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/group-buying.jpg)

Some live streaming apps display animated notifications when a user sends a tip — "So-and-so sent a gift" — turning a private payment into a public act, making other users more susceptible to conformity pressure.

![](/content/product-psychology-conformity/live-streaming-tip.jpg)

### 6. Prior Commitment

Once an individual makes a public commitment, they tend to stick with it and resist changing their conforming behavior. Public commitments make it difficult to back down.

This is why diving or gymnastics judges rarely change their scores, even when they realize their rating differs significantly from the other judges'.

This also means that if we want users to pay out of conformity, we should avoid giving them the chance to make a public assertion of non-purchase. In marketing, salespeople rarely ask directly, "Do you want it?" or "Will you buy it?" This avoids pushing the customer into a binary "no" with no room for flexibility.

## Let's Summarize

People have a natural tendency to conform — partly to gain group acceptance, and partly to avoid making wrong decisions in unfamiliar situations. The pressure to conform can sometimes be so intense that people make judgments that go against their own better knowledge.

By leveraging the conformity effect, we can create a purchasing environment — for example by using paid reviewers, running advertisements, creating scarcity marketing, etc. — that triggers a strong impulse to buy.

Many factors influence the effectiveness of conformity. To increase its impact, we can expand group size, reduce inconsistency, leverage friend recommendations, use high-status individuals as role models, create public response scenarios, and avoid letting people make prior negative commitments.

By making full use of these conformity principles and techniques, selling your products will no longer be a concern.

The conformity effect helps us build a buying environment — such as hiring paid commentators, running ads, creating hype — and thus create a strong purchase impulse in users.

To increase the influence of conformity, expand the group size, minimize dissenting voices, leverage friend referrals, use high-status individuals as examples, let users give feedback in public settings, and prevent them from making negative commitments in advance.

Full use of the above conformity principles and techniques will ensure product sales.

**Think about it: Can you think of other examples where the conformity effect is being used?**
