$200K salary → $0. Savings: Burned through $87K. Reality: Building products is hard. Twitter told me it was easy.

March 2023. I handed in my resignation at Google.

$220,000 total comp. Stock vesting. Comfortable life.

I was going to build a SaaS. Twitter made it look easy.

"Just solve a problem you have." "Ship fast, iterate faster." "Build in public, the customers will come."

18 months later.

Monthly Recurring Revenue: $147. Savings burned: $87,000. Salary lost: $300,000+. Lessons learned: Priceless.

Here’s the month-by-month breakdown of how I went from “crushing it” to crushing my bank account.

## Before I Quit: The Delusion

I spent 6 months reading Twitter threads.

Every indie hacker success story:

- “Hit $10K MRR in 3 months”
- “Quit my job, now making $50K/month”
- “Passive income while I sleep”

My brain did the math:

If they can do it in 3 months, I can too. I’m a senior engineer at Google. I know how to build things.

My wife: “Are you sure about this?”

Me: “I’ve got 12 months of savings. I’ll be profitable in 6.”

Narrator: He would not be profitable in 6 months.

## Month 1: The Excitement Phase

pril 2023.

First Monday without a job. Woke up at 10 AM. No standup. No meetings. Pure freedom.

I was going to build a SaaS for developers. Something I needed. Problem I understood.

The idea: Automated code review tool.

Market research: 30 minutes of Google searches. Competitor analysis: Looked at 3 other tools, thought “I can build better.” Validation: Asked 5 developer friends. 4 said “sounds cool.”

That was enough validation for me.

Spent the entire month coding.

Hours worked: 60–70/week. Lines of code written: 8,400. Customer interviews: 0. Marketing: 0. Revenue: $0.

Savings: $87,000 → $82,500.

## Month 2–3: The Building Phase

May-June 2023.

I was in the zone. Coding 12 hours a day.

Built:

- Authentication system
- GitHub integration
- Static code analysis engine
- Dashboard UI
- Pricing page

It was beautiful. Clean architecture. Well-tested. Scalable.

My wife: “When are you going to get customers?”

Me: “Once it’s perfect.”

Twitter told me: Ship fast.

I shipped slow. Because I was a perfectionist.

Customer interviews: Still 0. Beta testers: 0. Email list: 0.

Savings: $82,500 → $73,000.

## Month 4: The Launch

July 2023.

Product Hunt launch day.

I’d prepared everything:

- Landing page (rewrote it 7 times)
- Demo video (spent 3 days on it)
- Launch tweet (crafted for 2 hours)
- Posted at 12:01 AM PT. Perfect timing.

Results:

- Product Hunt upvotes: 47
- Website visitors: 320
- Sign-ups: 12
- Paying customers: 0

I launched on a Tuesday. Everyone says launch on Wednesday.

That must be why it failed.

Savings: $73,000 → $68,500.

## Month 5: The Pivot (First One)

August 2023.

12 sign-ups, 0 paying customers.

I asked the sign-ups for feedback.

3 responded:

- “Interesting but we use GitHub Actions”
- “Too expensive for what it does”
- “Doesn’t integrate with our stack”

I decided to pivot.

New idea: Developer productivity tracking tool.

Why: “Because I need this myself.”

Spent the month rebuilding.

Previous code: 8,400 lines. Thrown away.

Savings: $68,500 → $64,000.

## Month 6: The Reality Check

September 2023.

My wife got pregnant.

Her: “Maybe you should get a job?”

Me: “I’m close to product-market fit. Just need a few more months.”

Her: “You said that 2 months ago.”

She was right.

Launched v2 of the product. Developer productivity tracking.

Results:

- Sign-ups: 8
- Paying customers: 1
- Monthly revenue: $29
- My first dollar of MRR.

I celebrated by working 14 hours that day.

Savings: $64,000 → $59,000.

## Month 7–8: The Grind

October-November 2023.

I started marketing. Finally.

Tried everything Twitter told me to try:

- Twitter threads (engagement: 14 likes average)
- Reddit posts (banned from 2 subreddits for “self-promotion”)
- Cold email (0.4% response rate)
- Paid ads (spent $2,400, got 3 sign-ups)
- Customer count: 3 paying. MRR: $87 ($29 + $29 + $29).

Realized I priced too low. Couldn’t raise prices. Only had 3 customers.

Savings: $59,000 → $49,000.

## Month 9: The Burnout

December 2023.

I was tired.

Working 70 hour weeks. Making $87/month.

Did the math:

Hourly rate: $0.31/hour.

My wife’s comment: “The McDonalds near us is hiring at $18/hour.”

She wasn’t wrong.

Applied to 8 jobs. Got 3 interviews. No offers.

Why: 9-month gap on resume. “What were you doing?”

Me: “Building a SaaS.”

Them: “How’s that going?”

Me: “It’s… growing.” ($87/month)

Savings: $49,000 → $44,000.

## Month 10–11: The Second Pivot

January-February 2024.

Decided the product was wrong again.

New idea: API monitoring tool.

Why: Saw a tweet about someone making $40K/month with an API monitoring tool.

Threw away the productivity tracking tool.

Built API monitoring in 6 weeks.

Previous products abandoned: 2. Code thrown away: 14,000+ lines. Lessons learned: Unclear.

Savings: $44,000 → $34,000.

## Month 12: The Breakthrough (That Wasn’t)

March 2024.

One year since I quit.

Launched API monitoring tool.

Got my first big customer. A startup doing 500K requests/day.

They signed up for $99/month plan.

MRR: $87 → $186.

I thought: “This is it. This is the inflection point.”

Narrator: It was not the inflection point.

That customer churned after 1 month. They built their own solution.

Back to: $87/month.

Savings: $34,000 → $28,000.

## Month 13–14: The Panic

April-May 2024.

Savings dropping below $30K. Baby coming in 3 months.

Started freelancing. $80/hour contract work.

Kept working on SaaS at night.

Hours on SaaS: Dropped to 10–15/week. Hours on freelance: 30–35/week. Revenue from freelance: $10,400. Revenue from SaaS: $87.

I was working 2 jobs to keep building a product that made $87/month.

Savings: $28,000 → $22,000 (but freelance kept it from dropping further).

## Month 15: The VC Pitch

June 2024.

A friend introduced me to a VC.

VC asked about traction.

Me: “$147/month MRR. Growing.”

(I’d gotten 2 more customers at $29 each.)

VC: “What’s your burn rate?”

Me: “Uh… I’m solo, so just living expenses. About $4,500/month.”

VC: “So you’re burning $4,500 to make $147?”

Me: “…yes, but I have a plan for — “

VC: “Good luck.”

The meeting lasted 12 minutes.

Savings: $22,000 → $17,000.

## Month 16–17: The Acceptance

July-August 2024.

I accepted reality.

This was not going to be a $10K/month SaaS.

This was not going to replace my Google salary.

This was a $147/month side project that cost me $87,000 in savings and $300,000+ in lost salary.

But I kept building.

Why? Sunken cost fallacy. Pride. Stubbornness.

Also: I’d learned more in 16 months than I learned in 4 years at Google.

Just not how to make money.

Savings: $17,000 → $8,000.

## Month 18: Today

September 2024.

MRR: $147 ($29 × 5 customers, one on the $32 plan).

Savings: $8,000.

Baby: Born last month.

Wife’s job: Covering all our expenses.

My freelance work: $12K/month now.

My SaaS: Still running. Still making $147/month.

I’m not shutting it down. Because of pride? Maybe. Because I still believe? A little.

Mostly because I want to turn this failure into a lesson.

## What Twitter Didn’t Tell Me

witter indie hackers made it look easy.

Here’s what they didn’t mention:

### 1. Survivorship bias is real.

For every “I hit $10K MRR in 3 months” tweet, there are 1,000 people making $0.

They don’t tweet about it.

### 2. “Build something you need” is terrible advice.

I needed an automated code review tool.

Turns out: I was the only one willing to pay for it.

### 3. Product-market fit is not “just iterate.”

I iterated 3 times. Threw away 14,000+ lines of code.

Still didn’t find product-market fit.

Because I never talked to customers before building.

### 4. Marketing is harder than coding.

I’m a great engineer. I built 3 solid products.

I’m a terrible marketer. I got 5 total paying customers.

Code doesn’t market itself.

### 5. Savings disappear faster than you think.

$87,000 felt like a lot.

18 months later: $8,000 left.

Burn rate is invisible until you’re almost out.

## What I Should Have Done

If I could go back to March 2023:

Don’t quit your job.

Build the SaaS while employed. Yes, it’s slower. But you’re not burning savings.

**Talk to 100 potential customers BEFORE writing code.**

I talked to 0 before building v1. 3 after building v1. 0 before building v2.

I built solutions looking for problems.

**Charge more.**

$29/month is a hobby price. Serious businesses pay $99-$299/month.

My low price signaled: “This isn’t serious software.”

**Focus on one product for 12 months.**

I built 3 products in 18 months.

If I’d stuck with v1 and just marketed it, I’d know if it could work.

**Validate the market, not the idea.**

I validated that people thought the idea was “cool.”

I didn’t validate that people would pay monthly for it.

## The Numbers Don’t Lie

Let’s do the brutal math.

**Income lost:**

- Google salary (18 months): ~$330,000  
- Stock vesting: ~$80,000  
- 401k match: ~$15,000  
- Benefits value: ~$20,000  
- Total lost: ~$445,000

**Costs:**

- Savings burned: $87,000  
- Tools/services: $4,200  
- Paid ads (failed): $3,800  
- Total spent: $95,000

**Revenue:**

- MRR: $147/month  
- 18 months total: ~$1,200  

**Net outcome: -$538,800**

I paid $538,800 to learn how not to build a SaaS.

## But Here’s What I Gained

I’m not going to pretend this was worth it financially.

It wasn’t.

But I learned:

- How to validate ideas before building
- How to talk to customers (finally)
- How to market (badly, but I learned)
- How to handle rejection (VC, customers, Reddit moderators)
- How to ship fast (learned this in month 14, should’ve learned it in month 1)

And I learned about myself:

- I’m a better engineer than entrepreneur
- I need structure (Google’s structure kept me productive)
- I’m risk-averse (should never have quit without validation)
- I’m stubborn (kept building for 18 months despite clear failure)

These lessons cost $538,800.

Expensive lessons.

## What I’m Doing Now

I’m not shutting down the SaaS.

$147/month covers the hosting ($40/month) and tools ($60/month).

Net profit: $47/month.

I work on it 2–3 hours/week. Mostly bug fixes.

Freelancing: $12K/month. Enough to rebuild savings.

Applying to jobs: Senior roles at startups. $180K-$200K range.

I’ll probably take a job in the next 2 months.

The “indie hacker dream” is over.

But I’m not bitter. I tried. I failed. I learned.

That’s more than most people who dream about quitting can say.
