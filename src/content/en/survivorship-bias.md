## I. Origin of the Term

Survivorship bias comes from World War II.

Aircraft and pilots were precious combat assets, and the US bombers played a critical role in the war. The military took aircraft safety seriously, assembling a team of scientists to study how to improve survival rates.

They surveyed all the planes in the hangar, examining where returning aircraft had been hit. Military high command analyzed the bullet holes on every plane that made it back.

They found that the wings were densely peppered with bullet holes, while the fuselage and tail sections had far fewer. The Allied high command's initial recommendation: **reinforce the wings.**

But a Columbia University statistician on the team — **Abraham Wald** — rejected this. He argued the exact opposite: reinforce the fuselage and tail.

His reasoning: the planes in the hangar were the survivors — the ones that made it back. Some had been hit multiple times in the wings and fuselage and still returned safely, proving those areas could take damage.

The tail, engine, and cockpit sections had few or no bullet holes — not because they weren't hit, but because planes hit there never made it back to the hangar. They were simply not in the data.

Wald insisted on reinforcing the engine and tail sections. The course of the war proved him right.

In statistics, survivorship bias is defined as: **when we ignore the randomness and completeness of the sample during analysis, substituting a local subset for the full random sample, causing a distorted description of the population.**

In plain English: "You're only looking at the traits of the survivors — that doesn't give you a convincing conclusion." **At its core, survivorship bias is a cognitive error caused by incomplete information.**

## II. Survivorship Bias in Everyday Life

Plenty of everyday examples illustrate survivorship bias. Let's walk through three levels — easy, medium, and hard.

### 1. Easy Level

**1)** Online stores selling extreme sports gear — especially parachutes and paragliders — have nothing but five-star reviews. Zero negative reviews.

**Explanation:** The only people who can leave reviews are those who used the product and survived. The ghosts who'd leave bad reviews can't exactly type.

**2)** Your mom cooks at home every day, and you've lived with her for decades. You notice she's never picky about food.

**Explanation:** She buys only what she likes when grocery shopping. The food samples are pre-screened from the start. Of course she doesn't complain.

**3)** "Old things in the house work great. New products are terrible — they break in days! Manufacturers these days have no conscience!"

**Explanation:** The stuff that didn't work well was thrown out long ago. What remains is the stuff that lasted.

### 2. Medium Level

**"Other people's wives are better." (Attributed to Japanese author Junichi Watanabe)**

This has two layers of explanation.

**Layer 1**

Your wife sees you as her closest person and shows you her real self at home: no makeup, loud yawns, picking her nose, passing gas.

But "other people's wives" — the version you see — is always made up, well-dressed, and careful with words and posture. Naturally, they seem better.

**The illusion arises because the sample isn't random — different times, places, and contexts skew the average.**

**Layer 2**

The human brain is wired to remember extremes. The more beautiful someone is, the more they stand out. The uglier they are, the more shocking and memorable. The vast middle — ordinary-looking people — don't get remembered.

We don't form strong impressions of average-looking "other people's wives." But the beautiful, elegant ones stick in memory.

Objectively, under the single variable of appearance, less attractive people face more difficulty marrying. So most married women tend to be more attractive.

Thus, your memory of "other people's wives" is mostly beautiful, outstanding, and elegant — another case of survivorship bias.

### 3. Hard Level: "Successology" (The Cult of Success)

This extends to toxic feminist takes, "education is useless" arguments, anecdotal experience worship, and get-rich-quick entrepreneurship myths.

Believers in "successology" are everywhere these days, with some even founding their own schools of thought.

There's the "celebrity successology" pushed by Jack Ma, Pony Ma, Steve Jobs, Bill Gates, and others. There's the "self-sacrifice successology" of people sharing their own rise stories. And there's the pyramid-scheme version peddled by Chen Anzhi and his ilk — including the infamous "prodigy" Cen Yinuo who supposedly wrote 2000 poems a day.

Airport bookstores stock successology on their front shelves — a testament to its popularity. The reason is simple: it exploits people's desire for quick results without real effort.

Successology obsessively describes the behaviors and mindsets of successful people — especially the traits that supposedly "made" them — while rarely mentioning the root causes of their success. This creates a multilayered survivorship bias trap.

**Take Steve Jobs as an example.**

From successology books, you learn about Jobs's life: born to a blue-collar family, college dropout, bold investor, passionate visionary, obsessed with electronics, hungry for money and success.

From peers and business leaders, you hear about his technical brilliance, sharp business mind, and unprecedented innovative thinking and aesthetic sense — all of which drove his success.

Readers — especially those who didn't study hard in school, failed to get into a good university, regret it but lack motivation to restart, or nouveau riche trying to hold onto their wealth — feel like success is suddenly within reach.

They get pumped up, feel like they've found the truth, and the books sell even better.

The end result: successology believers form their own survivorship bias — the bias they now firmly hold.

**They believe the traits described in successology books are the golden keys to wealth.** The inconvenient traits that authors deliberately filtered out — those are the ones that actually matter.

Ironically, the traits readers embrace might help a little, or they might backfire.

For example, Jobs's dropping out of college could mislead many — because the probability of a dropout becoming successful is far lower than for graduates (yet this exact point is used to push "education is useless" narratives).

The essence of successology: people with ulterior motives exploit human nature, feeding one-sided but true information. Half-truths carefully curated to manufacture survivorship bias — all to harvest profits.

Whenever you hear someone's "success formula," ask yourself whether survivorship bias is at play. Instead of reading successology, pick up a real textbook — that's what will actually improve your abilities.

Similar traps exist everywhere: toxic feminist rhetoric, "education is useless," "chicken soup for the soul," stock market "wisdom," entrepreneurship myths.

These "theories" lack rigorous logic. They're placebos for the weak, not reflections of reality. Believing them long-term leads to flawed thinking and atrophied reasoning skills.

Stay alert when someone tries to sell you this nonsense. Pause and think.

Take a cancer patient told that praying to a god will save them — because "the guy in the next village prayed every day and was cured."

The reality: plenty of cancer patients who prayed and still died are in no position to speak.

## III. How to Avoid Survivorship Bias

First, understand what causes it.

**Survivorship bias is fundamentally a logical and statistical fallacy.** It happens when we ignore sample randomness and completeness, substituting a partial sample for the full one.

Three conditions typically produce it:

① **Sampling is involved.** If we could examine everything and know the full truth, there'd be no bias.

② **The population is unevenly distributed.** If the distribution were uniform, sampling would usually be unbiased.

③ **One or more subgroups with different distributions are excluded from the sampling frame**, as illustrated:

![Sampling Bias Diagram](/content/survivorship-bias/sampling-bias.png)

Example: a teacher who always calls on students in the front rows to check if the class understands the material.

Students in the back rows are typically the ones struggling or not paying attention. The teacher never calls on them — so the assessment is itself affected by survivorship bias.

**Survivorship bias also exploits human nature.**

We focus on outcomes while barely noticing the process that produced them.

The information we rely on comes mostly or entirely from "visible data." We rarely consider the "invisible data" — and often ignore "silent data" entirely.

The filtered-out information is either something we wouldn't notice anyway, or something deliberately hidden.

Bad data → bad conclusions → survivorship bias.

Many survivorship bias traps are subtle and highly damaging — precisely because they exploit human weakness.

**Survivorship bias can't be fully eliminated. But we can reduce the damage it causes.** Here are three suggestions:

### 1. Pay Attention to "Silent" Data

First, you need to *know* that silent evidence exists — only then can you form a more complete picture.

If you spend all your time on WeChat Moments and Douyin, you might think buying designer bags, fine dining, and overseas travel is the new Chinese normal.

Then Pinduoduo exploded — and the "silent evidence" roared: people buying cheap goods and mobilizing friends to save a few cents — *that's* the real Chinese mainstream.

Hearing isn't believing. Seeing isn't always believing either. Break your habitual thinking. Look past the obvious data to what's hiding behind it.

### 2. Learn Math. Learn Statistics.

Fund companies proudly announce that the industry's average return over the past decade exceeded 100%. Sounds like a sure thing, right?

If you know math, you'd spot the problem immediately: they're only counting funds *still alive* today. The ones that lost money and died were never included.

Factor in the dead ones, and the average return looks thoroughly mediocre.

### 3. Raise Your Cognitive Level

Survivorship bias often persists simply because your cognitive level is limited. You only know the surface information — you're not even aware that critical information exists. So you make bad calls.

Keep learning. Broaden your horizons. Expand your cognitive range.

In practice: before making any decision, try to gather information as comprehensively as possible. Think about it from both positive and negative angles.

Heaven envies geniuses — because no one cares how long fools live. Beautiful women die young — because no one cares how long plain women live.

Not every Hollywood movie is a blockbuster. The bad ones never get imported.

Not every entrepreneur succeeds. The failures never get attention.

Banlangen (Radix Isatidis) can't cure everything. And struggling students rarely become outliers.

Stop worshiping survivors. Behind every story you hear, countless others are crying in silence.

Understand survivorship bias, and amid the noise about miracle cures, you'll hear the silent victims.

Understand survivorship bias, and amid the crowd's frenzy for charlatans, you'll seek the hidden truth.

Understand survivorship bias, and you won't just see Jack Ma's "one general's glory" — you'll also see the "ten thousand bones" of entrepreneurs who paved the way.
