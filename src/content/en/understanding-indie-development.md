In the past two years, the concept of indie development has become incredibly popular — podcasts, online courses, apps, and more. Many tech professionals have created impressive works in their spare time. The reasons behind this phenomenon lie primarily in the transformation of production relations within the internet industry: the means of production provided by companies are no longer a constraint for knowledge workers, and offices are no longer necessary spaces for information exchange. With the help of increasingly abundant and sophisticated internet tools, an individual or a small team of two or three can create a commercially viable product and generate income.

Take the transportation industry as an example. Ride-hailing drivers don't need to sign labor contracts with any single company. To maximize their own profits, they can work across multiple platforms simultaneously, choosing whichever platform has customers and offers higher prices. Compare this with traditional taxi drivers, who must work for a specific taxi company — besides the cost of buying the vehicle, they also have to pay monthly management fees and are subject to company regulations. The means of production (the vehicle) functions as an asset for ride-hailing drivers — they use their own car through platforms to maximize their earnings. For taxi drivers, however, the vehicle functions as a liability — they must meet monthly quotas, bear the opportunity cost of not being able to drive for ride-hailing services, and only keep what remains after all that.

Furthermore, AI as a new form of labor has already begun embedding itself into specific roles across various industries. Over 90% of the marketing calls you receive today are AI-automated — they recommend products based on your responses, and it's nearly impossible to tell them apart from humans by voice alone. Looking at the call center industry, it becomes clear that many repetitive, low-value, knowledge-light jobs have already entered the scope of AI replacement. In other words, roles that rely entirely on company-provided means of production are at high risk of being replaced by AI.

In this environment, tech professionals are uniquely advantaged. First, mobile internet has entered a plateau phase where scenarios are fragmenting, and many niches remain unexplored — ideal for optimizing specific experiences. Second, knowledge spillover means that technology serves as productivity, and the workplace provides continuous opportunities for improvement along with social connections — individuals no longer naturally degrade over time. Third, the country's digital infrastructure provides the fundamental conditions for user experience.

As a result, indie developers can create a product with minimal cost — even just using spare time, a single computer, and the capabilities of mature internet platforms. With the right conditions, they can even achieve rapid monetization.

Our parents' generation faced the layoffs brought by China's reform and opening-up. In their era, there was no mature business environment, no internet, no niche markets. In such a coarse environment, individuals had no real choice — they had to rely on their employers to face market changes collectively. That's why they pursued stability and endurance — for stable social relationships and inner peace.

In the world reshaped by the internet, even those still employed need to deeply understand that for indie developers, the significance of a company is merely as an intermediary between the individual and the market. This intermediary only gives knowledge workers who can't directly face the market an opportunity to provide value to it. On the other hand, companies integrate and package their employees' capabilities into products. When the market pays, the company takes a portion and distributes some to employees, keeping the rest as compensation for risk-bearing stakeholders. In other words, your earnings are largely taken as "fees" by the company acting as an intermediary. What should be your income is legally transferred to risk-takers through labor contracts — nothing more.

The meaning of the company becomes quite pale once you understand its role. The mindset of relying on a company for lifelong security is no longer reliable, and it's hard to predict whether a single skill set will still be employable under the impact of AI. Our parents' way of thinking has been shattered by the internet.

The greatest significance of indie development lies in facing the market directly, contacting users directly, getting first-hand information, and earning what you truly deserve. Indie development is essentially similar to a company. A company's core is to directly face the market and sell its capabilities. Without a company as an intermediary, indie development means using your own abilities as a product to bring value to the market.

Indie development is not a revolution in production relations — it is an adaptation of production relations in response to major environmental changes. It is the subversion of the corporate system in the age of knowledge.

This makes it easy to understand why Naval Ravikant says in *The Almanack of Naval Ravikant* that the best business environment in the world is seven billion people running seven billion companies.

You might think indie development is difficult. That's because you haven't yet realized the uniqueness of your background. Your story and your combination of skills are unique in the entire world. Once you recognize your uniqueness, your story will find its audience, and there will be a market. The rest is just how you tell your story and how you serve your users.

Think back: who decides what you work on at your job? Who scores your performance? Who is responsible for your work process? Clearly, the company cannot actually intervene in your work process. All it can do is use systems and performance reviews as carrots, using bonuses as short-term incentives that cause employees to lose long-term vision. In a space full of "junk time" and bullshit jobs, people are constantly PUA'd and drained, only to be discarded like waste by the company when they turn 35.

Think about ride-hailing drivers again. Setting aside social prejudices, they maximize their own profits by offering multiple services simultaneously, slicing their time, free from traditional work ethics, without a company as an intermediary. They work for themselves, hedging against the negative impact of AI. Isn't this the most beautiful future for knowledge workers — especially those in tech?
