> When selling a product, you can choose to tell a great product story, then package its value — which can multiply its perceived worth several times over (for non-standard products with no brand recognition) — and finally close the deal with smart sales tactics.

When selling products, we often face this pain: My product quality is excellent, but nobody buys it. Prices have been slashed below cost, yet customers still complain it's too expensive. We've tried every promotion tactic, but sales are still dismal.

I believe many businesses struggle with these issues. To solve them, we need to learn the art of value packaging.

Value packaging involves three steps.

## Value Packaging Step 1: Tell a Good Story

If your product has a story behind it, customers will unconsciously become both users and evangelists. Take Chu Shijian's inspirational oranges — by telling Chu's story, they became a major brand with massive sales. Swap in bananas instead of oranges, still with Chu Shijian, and you'd get "inspirational bananas" with the same effect.

![Story Example](/content/value-packaging-three-steps/story-example.png)

In the bottled water world, there's a brand called Evian, selling for ¥18 a bottle. Here's the story they packaged:

> In 1789, during the French Revolution, a French aristocrat named Marquis de Lessert was suffering from kidney stones. At death's door and on the verge of despair, he happened to wander into the town of Évian-les-Bains. He drank some spring water from the Cachat family's garden, and after a period of drinking it, he was astonished to find that his illness had miraculously healed.

Armed with this story of miraculous spring water, Evian natural mineral water now sells in over 140 countries and regions worldwide. Evian isn't just selling a bottle of water — it's selling a philosophy, a lifestyle. When you drink Evian, you're tasting the meltwater from the Alps, naturally filtered and mineralized through glacial sand layers, transformed into pure, natural, nourishing water — something no other mineral water can offer.

Those are big-brand story packages. But what everyday products can also be packaged through stories?

- Aunt Wang next door was laid off at 50. With quiet resilience, she shouldered her family's burdens. Through decades of hard work and wisdom, she devoted herself to making the perfect pancake for her customers.
- Beekeeping master Mr. Zhao started keeping bees in his late teens, living deep in the mountains. With 40 years of dedication and sincerity, he brings you nature's finest flavors.

By digging into the stories behind a company or product, you can "package" a great narrative. It can be touching, sentimental, fun — or even manufactured (like Li Chen's heart-shaped stone incident).

## Value Packaging Step 2: Package a Good Product

A good story alone isn't enough — the product quality itself must be solid. Only a truly good product, combined with a great story, can drive sales.

We can package the product around these angles:

### 1. Package Through Product Features

Treadmills weren't selling — rebrand them as weight-loss machines and they became a smash hit. Call the steel running deck "sturdy enough to handle any body type"; the heart rate handgrips become "for safer weight loss"; the five-layer running belt is "every step burns double the calories"; the quiet energy-efficient motor is "so you don't disturb the neighbors during your workout." The large running surface, built-in fan, and front handgrips all become "for comfortable weight loss" — even change the metrics from kilometers to calories burned…

### 2. Package Through "Craftsmanship"

Tech products can highlight premium raw materials, complex manufacturing processes (18 steps of craftsmanship), internationally leading technology, and billions in R&D investment — conveying a sense of high-tech sophistication to consumers.

Leather bags can claim that one cow only makes three bags, letting users feel elevated and emotionally fulfilled.

Beekeeping masters can walk through the entire process of raising bees and producing honey, emphasizing the complexity of the craft, the uniqueness of the process, the various value-added ingredients in the honey, and its distinctive functions and benefits — differentiating it from ordinary honey on the market.

### 3. Package Through "Product Packaging"

Mooncakes get exquisite gift boxes, elevating the overall product value. The product itself doesn't change, but the price multiplies several times over — because consumers need that premium look when gift-giving.

## Value Packaging Step 3: Create a Good Sales Strategy

Tell a good story, package a good product — now customers want to buy from you. The next step is getting them to commit and pay quickly: leverage human psychology for both online and offline sales packaging.

The love of beauty is universal. Products can always look better — bright product images, highlighted core features, unique photography angles. If you're selling women's clothing with great quality at reasonable prices, hire a local female model, shoot a series of high-end photos, and the perceived value instantly skyrockets.

Play on fear and desire: show them the downsides of not using it, the benefits of using it. Write copy that taps into human nature. Beauty salon — without it, the husband loses interest and leaves; with it, the husband adores you and buys you bags. Without it, your life is bleak; with it, your life is transformed. Before-and-after comparisons make everyone want to become their best self.

Leverage herd mentality, authority, and scarcity: high sales volumes draw followers, expert endorsements build trust, celebrity posts boost prestige. Limited quantities (only 100 sets nationwide), limited time (pay within 24 hours), limited buyers (one per person). Clothing brands create buzz, pile on English and French labels, and claim the product is already a hit overseas before even launching domestically.

Tap into the desire for gain: package imported gifts that cost ¥2-3 as ¥20-30 value, showing "great sincerity." Gifts should be practical items people carry daily — keychains, plush toys, cups, mouse pads, etc.

There are plenty of other sales models: free trials, free samples, member-only pricing, buy-one-get-ten deals, free delivery, free returns and exchanges…

When you tap into the seven deadly sins — greed, laziness, lust, envy, wrath, pride, gluttony — any packaging that drives sales is good packaging.

To summarize: Tell a great product story, then package its value — this can multiply its perceived worth several times over (for non-standard products with no brand recognition) — and then close the deal through smart sales strategies.
