Backlinks (also known as inbound links, incoming links) are links from one website's page to another website. Search engines like Google use backlinks as a signal for webpage rankings.

![What are backlinks](/content/what-are-backlinks/what-are-backlinks-intro.png)

## Why Backlinks Matter

Backlinks are important for SEO for two main reasons:

1. **Ranking** — Generally, the more backlinks a webpage has, the higher it tends to rank for relevant search queries (we confirmed this in a [study](https://ahrefs.com/blog/search-traffic-study/)).
2. **Discoverability** — Search engines crawl popular pages more frequently than unpopular ones. If you get backlinks from popular pages, search engines may discover your content faster.

New to link building? Check out our [link building guide for beginners](https://ahrefs.com/blog/link-building/).

## How to Check Backlinks

There are two ways to check backlinks for a website or webpage.

The first method only works for websites you own. The second method lets you check backlinks for any website or webpage.

### Check Backlinks for Your Own Site

The most basic tool for checking your website's backlinks is Google Search Console.

After logging in, click "Links" in the sidebar. The number under "External links" shows your website's total unique backlinks count.

![How to check backlinks in Google Search Console](/content/what-are-backlinks/gsc-check-backlinks.png)

Google Search Console shows limited data (top 1,000 links) and doesn't display useful SEO metrics for analyzing your backlinks. To get more data for free, you can use [Ahrefs Webmaster Tools](https://ahrefs.com/webmaster-tools).

After setting up a project, click **Backlinks** on the dashboard.

![Backlinks summary in Ahrefs dashboard](/content/what-are-backlinks/ahrefs-backlinks-summary.png)

This takes you to the **Backlinks** report in [Site Explorer](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer). The report shows all your backlinks and related data.

![Viewing backlink data in Ahrefs Site Explorer](/content/what-are-backlinks/ahrefs-site-explorer-backlinks.png)

### Check Backlinks for Another Site

You can start with Ahrefs' free [backlink checker](https://ahrefs.com/backlink-checker) tool.

Simply enter a domain or URL and click "Check backlinks."

![Ahrefs free backlink checker tool](/content/what-are-backlinks/ahrefs-backlink-checker.png)

You'll see the total number of backlinks and referring domains (links from unique websites), along with data for the top 100 backlinks.

![Backlink data](/content/what-are-backlinks/backlink-data-example.png)

To view the complete backlink list for any webpage or website, use Ahrefs [Site Explorer](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer).

## What Makes a Good Backlink?

Not all backlinks are created equal. Here are some attributes that affect backlinks. You can use them to analyze your backlinks and link building opportunities:

- Authority
- Relevance
- Anchor text
- Follow vs. nofollow
- Placement
- Destination

![What makes a good link](/content/what-are-backlinks/good-backlink-qualities.png)

### Authority

Backlinks from "strong" pages typically pass more "authority" than those from "weak" pages.

![Pages with backlinks have higher voting authority](/content/what-are-backlinks/page-authority-voting.png)

We've conducted multiple studies on page-level authority and found a clear relationship with organic traffic.

![URL Rating vs organic traffic](/content/what-are-backlinks/ur-vs-organic-traffic.png)

[URL Rating](https://help.ahrefs.com/en/articles/72658-what-is-ahrefs-url-rating) (UR) is Ahrefs' page-level authority metric. It's scored from 0 to 100.

That said, backlinks from authoritative pages don't always pass more authority. The more links a page has, the less authority passes to you, because authority is shared across all those links (the [PageRank](https://ahrefs.com/blog/google-pagerank/) algorithm).

You can measure backlink authority using the UR metric in the **Backlinks** report of Ahrefs [Site Explorer](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer). Higher UR is better.

![UR metric in Ahrefs backlinks report](/content/what-are-backlinks/ahrefs-ur-metric.png)

### Relevance

Links from websites on the same topic as yours are considered more valuable. Google points this out in its "How Search Works" guide:

> If other prominent websites on the subject link to the page, that's a sign that the information is of high quality.

Imagine a plumber has backlinks from two pages: one about cats and another about installing boilers. In this case, relevance means the latter link is likely more valuable.

![Backlinks from relevant pages are more valuable](/content/what-are-backlinks/relevance-backlinks-value.png)

### Anchor Text

Anchor text is the clickable text that makes up a backlink.

![Anchor text example](/content/what-are-backlinks/anchor-text-example.png)

Google stated in its [original patent](http://infolab.stanford.edu/~backrub/google.html#pr) that anchor text affects rankings.

> Google uses a number of techniques to improve search quality including page rank, anchor text, and proximity information.

You can view the anchor text of any backlink in the **Backlinks** report of [Site Explorer](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer).

![Viewing anchor text in Ahrefs Site Explorer](/content/what-are-backlinks/ahrefs-anchor-text.png)

You can also filter anchor text for specific words.

![Anchor text filter](/content/what-are-backlinks/anchor-text-filter.png)

### Follow vs. Nofollow

Nofollow is a link attribute that tells Google not to follow the link and hints not to pass authority (as of [2019](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2019/09/evolving-nofollow-new-ways-to-identify)).

![Nofollow link anatomy](/content/what-are-backlinks/nofollow-link-anatomy.png)

Follow links are those without this attribute, and without "sponsored" or "UGC" attributes.

Since nofollow links typically don't affect rankings, it's best to prioritize getting follow links. However, since "nofollow" is now just a hint, getting nofollow links from relevant, high-authority pages is still a good idea.

You can find these types of backlinks using the filters in the [Site Explorer](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer) **Backlinks** report.

![Filtering backlinks by rel attribute in Ahrefs](/content/what-are-backlinks/ahrefs-rel-attribute-filter.png)

**Further reading**

- [Nofollow vs Follow Links: Everything You Need to Know](https://ahrefs.com/blog/nofollow-links/)

### Placement

Since people are more likely to click links placed in prominent positions, some links on a page may carry more authority than others.

![Links in prominent positions pass more authority](/content/what-are-backlinks/link-placement-authority.png)

Bill Slawski discussed this in his [analysis](http://www.seobythesea.com/2016/04/googles-reasonable-surfer-patent-updated/) of Google's updated "reasonable surfer" patent:

> If a link is located in the main content area of a page, uses fonts and colors that might help it stand out, and uses text that might make it seem likely to be clicked, it passes a fair amount of PageRank. On the other hand, if it combines features that make it less likely to be clicked—like being in the footer, using the same color and font as the rest of the text, and using anchor text people aren't interested in—it might not pass much PageRank.

Keep this in mind when seeking links. If your link might end up in a site's footer or a sidebar alongside 50 other sites, it's better to focus your efforts elsewhere.

The "backlink type" filter in [Site Explorer](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer)'s **Backlinks** report can help you find backlinks placed within content (rather than less prominent positions).

![Filtering by backlink type in Ahrefs](/content/what-are-backlinks/ahrefs-backlink-type-filter.png)

### Destination

Since Google ranks pages, not entire websites, it's best to build links pointing directly to the page you want to rank.

However, getting links to certain page types can be difficult. For example, commercial pages are usually harder to get links to because people prefer linking to more informational content.

To solve this, you can use internal links to pass authority from pages that already have many links to important but "boring" pages.

![Using internal links to pass authority between pages](/content/what-are-backlinks/internal-links-authority.png)

## How to Get Backlinks

Generally, there are four ways to get more backlinks to your site:

![Four ways to get backlinks](/content/what-are-backlinks/four-ways-get-backlinks.png)

### Add Backlinks Manually

Some websites allow you to add links by manually submitting or requesting submission. This is an easy way to get links, but it's not always worthwhile. In Google's view, certain links may have low value, and if overused, they can even be considered spam.

One of the most common (and legitimate) strategies is adding your site to relevant directories. They can help you rank for locally-intended queries and help customers discover your business.

**Further reading**

- [How to Get Local Citations (Complete Guide)](https://ahrefs.com/blog/local-citations/)

### Ask for Backlinks

This involves contacting other website owners, editors, or webmasters to ask them to link to your page. For this to work, you need a clear value proposition. That's where link building strategies come in.

Here are several proven methods:

- [**Guest Blogging**](https://ahrefs.com/blog/guest-blogging/) — Writing articles for other websites.
- [**Broken Link Building**](https://ahrefs.com/blog/broken-link-building/) — Finding relevant broken links on other sites, then reaching out to suggest replacing them with your working links.
- [**Skyscraper Technique**](https://ahrefs.com/blog/skyscraper-technique/) — Finding related content with lots of backlinks, creating something better, then asking people to link to your content.
- [**Unlinked Mentions**](https://ahrefs.com/blog/unlinked-mentions/) — Finding sites that mention your brand without linking, then asking the author to add a link.

**Further reading**

- [9 Simple Link Building Strategies Anyone Can Use](https://ahrefs.com/blog/link-building-strategies/)
- [How to Get Backlinks: 7 Strategies That Don't Need New Content](https://ahrefs.com/blog/how-to-get-backlinks/)

### Buy Backlinks

Every so often, you'll come across offers to buy links — don't accept them.

Buying backlinks is extremely risky. Google strongly discourages it and your site could be penalized.

This shouldn't be confused with paid link building services. Good link building agencies use legitimate white-hat strategies that have nothing to do with spam or purchasing links.

**Further reading**

- [Should You Buy Backlinks in 2023?](https://ahrefs.com/blog/buy-backlinks/)

### Earn Backlinks Passively

This is when people discover your content through Google, social media, or word of mouth, and choose to link to your page. In other words, these backlinks happen naturally.

You can increase your chances of getting more backlinks by creating genuinely useful content that people want to link to. You should also promote your content. The more people you reach, the more links you're likely to earn.

## What to Avoid When Building Backlinks

Basically, when pursuing backlinks, avoid anything that looks low-quality, spammy, or like an obvious link scheme (such as link exchanges).

Bad links are a waste of time. At best, they won't affect your rankings. At worst, they could cause your pages to rank lower or not appear in Google at all.

![Google says sites violating its policies may rank lower or not appear](/content/what-are-backlinks/google-penalty-warning.png)

Here are some bad link types you should definitely avoid:

- **PBNs** — Multiple websites linked together to manipulate search engine rankings.
- **Paid Links** — Exchanging money, goods, or services for links to gain rankings.
- **Link Exchanges** — Linking to a site in exchange for them linking back to you.
- **Automated Links** — Using automated software or services to generate large volumes of links for your site.
- **Forum and Comment Spam** — Spam links in forum signatures or blog comments.
