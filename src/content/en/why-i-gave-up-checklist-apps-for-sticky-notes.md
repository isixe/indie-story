I can't remember when I started noticing it, but I've come to realize I'm someone who gets distracted easily.

Last weekend, I went to a new book sharing event, and it only confirmed this. I couldn't sit still — kept wanting to get up and move around. But since I was on someone else's turf, I had to keep my butt in the chair, either pulling out my phone or digging through my bag for articles I'd saved to read.

Maybe the content was dull, maybe my attention just wanders easily — but I wanted to do something about it. For someone easily distracted, the real problem isn't a lack of ideas. It's that when you actually need to focus on one thing, your brain goes into overdrive throwing out every thought imaginable: look something up, buy something, read an article.

I used to chase every thought. Halfway through writing an article, I'd wander off to YouTube or open a shopping app to check out some gadget I wanted — like the cat chasing butterflies in that elementary school textbook. Fine for personal stuff, but at work? That kind of distraction means overtime. The cost just isn't worth it.

As I was mulling over what tools might help, I absent-mindedly opened a drawer and a square sticky note pad caught my eye. The answer was right there.

**So I put a pad of sticky notes and a pen by my desk. Whenever a thought, idea, or random errand popped up, I'd jot it down, and handle it all later when I had free time. That way, I could stay focused on what was in front of me.**

The benefit of writing things down is simple: it releases the thought from your brain. You no longer have to hold it in working memory, so you can give your full attention to what matters. Our working memory is limited — about four chunks at a time. Even if you don't act on a thought, it lingers, chipping away at your focus.

This approach has a theoretical foundation too: GTD (Getting Things Done), developed by David Allen. Its core is a five-step workflow — capture, process, organize, review, and do. Sticky notes fit right into the capture and organize stages.

Sticky notes are a natural capture tool. Lightweight, small, free-form to write on, never runs out of battery, rock solid. When it's time to organize, you can sort them by color, type, or progress to get the full picture.

Beyond capturing ideas, sticky notes are great for to-dos. I still have an active annual subscription to TickTick, but I haven't opened it in three months. Every time I want to add a task, I have to correct the title, set priorities, add tags, assign groups — it feels like a chore. With sticky notes, I just write it down and stick it on the edge of my monitor. If I need to prioritize, I add a star.

![Sticky notes on the desk](/content/why-i-gave-up-checklist-apps-for-sticky-notes/desk-sticky-notes.jpg)

They're also a game-changer for reading. When you borrow a book and can't write in it, sticky notes give you freedom. Jot down quick thoughts, stick them on the page with the edge sticking out as a bookmark. When you're done reading, follow those notes and transfer your insights to a note-taking app.

![Sticky notes on a book](/content/why-i-gave-up-checklist-apps-for-sticky-notes/book-sticky-notes.jpg)

Sometimes I use them as a decision-making tool. I was recently considering whether to buy an iPhone (spoiler: I'd already decided not to), and I realized thinking it through endlessly was less effective than just laying it all out. So I wrote reasons for and against on sticky notes. In the end, all I could come up with was trying out a new system — was that really worth the money? From there, you can apply all kinds of mental models to sticky notes: pyramid structures, concept maps, SWOT analysis — anything goes when you're writing and drawing freely.

![To buy or not to buy iPhone — sticky notes](/content/why-i-gave-up-checklist-apps-for-sticky-notes/iphone-decision.jpg)

Of course, sticky notes aren't the only option. You can apply the same approach to other tools — a WeChat chat with yourself, a file-transfer assistant, or the built-in notes app on your phone. The key is immediacy.

I use sticky notes because they don't distract me. They're unassuming. The moment I open a phone app, I can't help scrolling through my feed, reading articles on WeChat Official Accounts — and before I know it, I've forgotten what I was even trying to do.

Their downside? You need a writing surface. If inspiration strikes while you're walking, it's awkward to pull out sticky notes. Voice recording in a notes app can actually help polish and clean up your thoughts.

Another flaw: the edges curl up. Sometimes they fall off the bottom of my monitor. Probably the adhesive isn't great — next time I'll try a different brand.

The more you write, the more issues pile up. What do you do when you have a heap of sticky notes scattered everywhere in chaos?

That's a sign you haven't processed them in time. Go through them one by one, or spread them out on the floor to sort. Ask yourself: is this still useful? If it's an unfinished task, consider whether to rewrite it on a fresh sticky note. If it's a passing thought, check if it made it into your notes. Once processed, the sticky note's job is done.

![Sticky notes scattered on the floor](/content/why-i-gave-up-checklist-apps-for-sticky-notes/scattered-sticky-notes.png)

But we get attached to things we've created — even hand-written sticky notes. So what to do? If you really want to keep them, use a paper notebook or a book as a staging area and stick them in there. If it's a book you've already read, flipping through the sticky notes later also serves as a nice refresher.

Then there's the question: why not just use AI? Whenever something comes to mind, just tell it to an AI and ask it later when you need to retrieve it.

Sticky notes are still the better choice. Besides, not every problem needs AI. When we rely on AI too much — asking it everything, accepting its answers without a hint of pushback — we're handing over our independent thinking. Just because it's "all-knowing" doesn't mean you don't have the right to think for yourself. Write it on a sticky note first, ask AI later when you have time. You might find you already had the answer before asking — or that the question itself wasn't even important.

At the end of the day, I started using sticky notes because I felt too distracted. Thoughts come and go like waves — deal with them and you interrupt what you're doing; ignore them and they hang over you, making it even harder to focus.

After a few days of practice, I'm convinced it works. It helps me protect my attention and my time — at the cost of nothing more than a few bucks for a pack of sticky notes.

**The outside world rushes in, and the mind churns like the tide. But a single little sticky note can be a speedboat, gliding freely across the sea of thought — and always bringing back what I need.**
