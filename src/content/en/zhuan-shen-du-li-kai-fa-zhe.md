## I

In the past two years, there's a popular line on social media: Don't curse your past self — he was standing in the fog back then, just as lost.

Every time I scroll past this, I mutter under my breath: And now? Are we not lost anymore? Not in the fog?

For most ordinary people, life can be summed up in two words.

**Survive. And live.**

Every trade has its own way of making a living. As someone who entered the internet industry back in 2016, I've experienced the halftime break of the industry and the quiet chill of its second half.

I landed my first internship by sheer memorization and a bit of luck, armed with just two months of Java from college, and charged straight into an internet company.

I remember when I first started working, a few classmates and I would often talk about career dreams.

Tucked away in some folder on a cloud drive, there's someone's ambitious ten-year plan. Whenever that friend wants to throw in the towel, I say:

**Want to take a look at that ten-year plan you wrote back in 2016?**

The truth is, the early days on the job are the hardest. When you're not good enough, you have to be cautious.

I was part of a development team of over a hundred people, where even the smallest mistake could cause huge trouble. I recall that at the start of the year, there were more than twenty interns, but half of them were gone before probation ended.

Most of them lost their jobs due to frequent small mistakes.

For programmers, the most common mistake was overwriting code. Next came genuinely not being able to write certain features. And after that, getting criticized once and calling it quits.

Even though I was clumsy as a newcomer, I had that youthful fearlessness back then. I always felt I was different.

**Time slipped away in Ctrl+C and Ctrl+V, and when I looked up, it was ten years later.**

By 2025, I'd learned to handle people and tasks at work with ease. I'd become a total veteran.

Ten years and more than three thousand days of experience and confusion — some I've nearly forgotten, some I wish I could delete, some I've turned into blog posts. Looking back on the changes over a decade, one sentence sums it up:

**Technology, business, career change — a three-way brawl.**

## II

Back in 2016, programming meant rote memorization. I could barely write basic CRUD, let alone talk about technical breadth or depth.

In that state, I naturally poured all my energy into building up my technical skills — studying code logic and design patterns in projects, copying someone else's code and modifying it to make it my own.

**Early-stage learning was nothing more than getting good at copy-paste-and-tweak.**

There's one critical factor in how fast and how deep a beginner learns technology: **it has a lot to do with your environment.**

At the time, I was in what you'd call a big company. They valued process and standards, and they put genuine effort into training interns.

For every feature we developed, a tech lead would walk us through the business logic and coding approach. After development, there'd be a code review and optimization, followed by thorough testing before release. Naturally, the tolerance for mistakes was very low.

Opportunity and pressure went hand in hand. Growing up in that kind of environment, I got a proper onboarding into the programming profession.

As a newcomer without much of a degree, landing a job at that kind of company from the start — I'm still grateful to that first company to this day. They paid well, didn't overwork me, and helped me build a solid foundation.

**"Paid well" means a starting salary of 5K RMB per month in 2016. So yes, that first company was the real deal.**

Looking back from where I stand now, there's a strange thing I can't quite explain: back then, I spent all my free time on technology. I naively believed that if I got good enough at tech, I could make a living anywhere.

There were plenty of tech veterans on the team, and I often heard them sigh that their careers were nearing the end.

I chose to ignore those voices. I never bothered to ask why. I just let a faint warning settle in the back of my mind — that in tech, there's a 35-year-old age crisis.

**Back then, I never imagined I'd be heading straight for that red line.**

## III

**Your career won't give you that much time to focus purely on technology.**

Companies that need pure tech talent usually have the money to hire people with real talent and experience. Most rank-and-file developers, after a year or two on the job, need to be able to stand on their own.

**And once that "one side" gets big enough, it can fold into many sides, forming a complete enclosure.**

Technology is just one inconspicuous piece of it. Business becomes the real tug-of-war at work. To truly stand on your own, you need a clear understanding of the business, and based on that, design a solid technical solution, and finally deliver a high-quality release.

For simple projects, you might pick up the business quickly thanks to a product manager's brief overview.

But in truly complex scenarios, you have to understand the industry's underlying model. The workflow extends down to the smallest details and is deeply logical. This accumulation takes time and effort, too.

In 2018, I joined a data company and witnessed its entire rise from zero.

In the early days, the company brought in a slew of top data experts from big tech. You couldn't let that resource go to waste — every day there was a small educational session, and on the sixth day of the 996 schedule, half the day was reserved for a big lecture.

Only one or two of them actually made sense in plain language. Most were just PPT jargon monologues. But the first time I truly understood a lecture, my expectations shifted. Because I saw how you could mine business value from data.

Giving up tech entirely was out of the question. But shifting most of my focus to business became my new direction.

So I started a period of deep accumulation in data, focusing on organizing, studying, and summarizing business logic, while also picking up plenty of technical solutions along the way. Multiple choices are definitely more fun than a single one.

**From then on, I was convinced: doing business well is no easier than doing tech well.**

When I parted ways with that data company, my mindset was at its peak. I'd worked with complex tech stacks, done massive data analysis, and had an unshakeable — if hard to articulate — confidence about my future career.

## IV

My second job hop in 2020 truly repaid the efforts of the previous years.

I landed at a company that was very good but not very big. Hard to imagine — the founder personally had deep knowledge of product, business, and technology, with a rare ability to orchestrate across all these dimensions.

He set the product direction from an industry-level vantage point, supporting business and technology to advance in sync.

When dealing with complex scenarios, he could provide the R&D team with guidance in terms of methods and case studies. The big principle was: get things done and do them well, without rushing.

**So what makes a good company? Just one that's a little bit normal.**

During my time there, I went through several business upgrades and completed the technical transformation of the system architecture, steadily pushing forward the company's digital transformation.

We built a complex business system without letting it turn into a total mess. In my entire career, this was the only company that managed that.

It was during my years at this company that I truly let go, mentally, of the so-called tech-versus-business dilemma. It wasn't some great epiphany — I just realized that getting things done means practicing whatever's needed.

**To use a trendy term: learning by doing.**

By 2023, the downward trend of the internet industry's second half was clear. Even though my combined technical and business skills were already well-honed, it was still just skill-level accumulation.

## V

The internet market after 2023 is a textbook case of "a speck of dust of an era that falls on each person's shoulders like a mountain."

As of 2025, I think the supply-demand-driven rat race in the internet industry has reached its peak.

Here's a reality: you post one development position, and you get 200-300 applicants saying hello every day. But the position might only need 1-2 people. Companies have a wide range of choices.

**Here's the question: Will those 2 be the best among the 200?**

The industry's downward trend affects both individuals and companies. These days, companies are undoubtedly making trade-offs based on cost-effectiveness — which is exactly why job seekers universally feel that luck plays a big role lately.

As a ten-year veteran of the internet, I've collided head-on with the tech industry's 35-year-old crisis.

I remember when we used to discuss startup projects in the early days of my career, we measured in three-year milestones. But in the last two years, the instability of the work environment and the three-month project cycle rule have all been enormous pressures bearing down on working people.

Through the uncertainty of these past two years, I've experienced long stretches of confusion and brief bouts of AI anxiety.

In the past decade, whether the time I invested was in technology or business, those were reasonable and appropriate choices at the time. But with the rapid iteration of large model capabilities, the bar for those so-called professional skills has been lowered again and again.

**I can get from 0 to 1 on my own. AI can quickly take 1 to 100.**

A term that's gained traction in the last couple of years: indie developer, one-person company, one-person unicorn. It sounds a bit pretentious, sure, but if you think about it, there's real truth to it.

The rat race in the internet industry and the rise of AI have made many developers feel that their investment in technology and business has been futile. For a long time, I felt the same way.

When indie development first caught fire last year, given the capabilities of large models at the time, I personally felt it was still unlikely — just another traffic topic for self-media.

Back then, large models weren't even at a usable level for professional tasks. How could you coordinate product development across different disciplines?

After DeepSeek exploded in 2025, I personally experimented with different models' capabilities in product development. I began to believe that AI could be a powerful assistant — even a teammate — in the development process.

Just half a year later, AI development tools are already trending toward replacing traditional tools.

**And so I turned around and set out on the indie developer path. I believe that teaming up with large models, I can build something both livable and fun.**

I'll document my attempts in this direction on my social media accounts. If you're interested, give me a follow. Let's explore together and find a way out of this unstable career.
